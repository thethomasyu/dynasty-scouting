# KJ Duff

*Rutgers. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Preseason read, expected to evolve. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

At some point this fall, a broadcast crew is going to look at KJ Duff, a legitimate 6'5"-and-change and 225 pounds, and call him a "matchup nightmare" on a five-yard hitch. It happens to every receiver this size, and it's usually wrong. College football produces a giant mismatch receiver every year, the NFL passes on almost every one of them, and the reason is simple: most huge college stars stop separating the moment everybody else on the field can run too.

Duff is this cycle's big guy, and the thing that stood out to me is that an unusual amount of his game is built to survive the stuff that normally kills this kind of receiver. His skill was never a question for me. What I keep going back and forth on is whether the job he's built for still produces NFL difference-makers, and whether he's the exception. I've also noticed that opinions on Duff tend to track how people already feel about giant receivers in general, so throughout this profile I'll try to be clear about which parts are actually him and which parts just come with the body type.

## How a receiver this size actually gets open

Duff gets open the way a power forward gets a rebound: the work happens before the ball goes up. He understands that he's bigger than everyone assigned to him, and he plays like a guy who never forgets it. He shields the ball with his frame. He walls defenders out of the catch space before the throw ever commits. He treats the intermediate middle of the field like his own personal property, and he knows exactly where the chains are while he's doing it.

Positional receiving is a real skill, and it's the one broadcast crews mistake for "just being tall." Being tall is just the raw material. Knowing how to arrive at the spot first, establish body position, and leave the defender playing through your back is craft, and Duff has it.

The production lines up with the way he plays. Over 1,000 yards last season at 18-plus a catch, half of his 60 receptions going for 15 or more, one monster game near 200 yards, and all of it through quarterback play inconsistent enough to make his weekly stat lines a coin flip. None of that is screen-and-scamper production. He was getting fed downfield and moving the chains.

## The hands

The best part of his game is honestly the least flashy thing he does: he catches like a receiver, not like a converted tight end.

Receivers his size almost always trap the ball against their chest. Nobody coaches it out of them, because when your catch radius is enormous, cradling works fine in college. Duff attacks the ball at full extension, plucks it away from his frame, and secures it before he starts running. At his size, full-extension catching expands the catchable area into places a defender physically cannot reach, and that's the difference between "big target" and a target you can throw to while he's covered.

I do have to be upfront about one thing here. On film, the hands are the part of his game I trust most. The technique is visible, and some of the grabs on his tape are among the most impressive in this class. The problem is that it's also the least documented part of his game: no reliable drop or contested-catch numbers are available yet. If those numbers eventually come in ugly, I'll rewrite this section. I don't expect that to happen, but I wanted to mention it anyway.

## So is he a good athlete?

Duff is not a fast receiver, but he moves shockingly well. Both of those things are true at the same time, so let me take them one at a time.

His long speed is ordinary. Nobody watches him and sees a sub-4.4 athlete. The realistic expectation lives in the mid-4.5s at best, and the NFL grades separation in absolute terms. Corners don't give him extra credit for his mass, and being "fast for his size" won't shake a nickel in man coverage.

The movement is the more interesting part. What usually kills giant receivers is stiffness, not the stopwatch. You see it in high hips that can't sink, long limbs that can't redirect, and routes that telegraph themselves because the body only bends one way. Duff doesn't have that problem. He's fluid, he has real wiggle, he runs respectable in-breakers, and 225 pounds arriving at his speed carries force a defensive back has to respect at the tackle point.

So no, the size context doesn't make him "athletic," and I refuse to grade it that way. What it changes is which risk applies to him. He mostly avoids the usual big-man failure mode, but he's fully exposed to the ordinary-speed one. He's in a better spot than most receivers his size ever get to be in, but the speed risk isn't going anywhere.

## The history working against him

There's no way around the track record here: receivers at 6'5"-plus with ordinary speed have a poor NFL hit rate as difference-makers. The league keeps drafting them on the theory that size is a skill, and most of them end up proving that separation is the skill and size is just a bonus.

The film itself leaves very little to argue about: the positioning is real, the hands are real, the movement is unusually clean for the mass. What's left to figure out is what that package is worth. I think he's the best current version of this type of receiver in the class, and the stiffness that sank so many of the guys before him just isn't in his game. Because of that, I'm giving him a real projection instead of marking him down for the body type.

That doesn't erase the risk, though. Now, what is the actual risk with him? It isn't that he busts. Even a skeptical read on Duff leaves an NFL starter, because chain-moving, red zone value, and reliable hands are employable everywhere. The risk is that he becomes catch-point dependent: a receiver who is never open in the geometric sense, only open in the sense that the ball can be placed where nobody else can reach it. That's a real NFL job, but the ceiling of that job belongs to the quarterback, because it only works with a passer accurate and trusting enough to throw receivers open. A catch-point receiver with a great quarterback looks like a star, but give the same receiver a scattershot passer and he disappears for a month at a time. Duff already lived that exact life at Rutgers last season, which is worth remembering in both directions.

## The nitpicks that matter

For an evaluation this clean, the flaws are pretty specific, and almost none of them are about ability. Most of them come down to how often he does things he's clearly capable of doing.

He drifts on downfield routes. Instead of holding his width and spacing, he lets his line bend toward the coverage, which shrinks the very window his size is supposed to create. There's a rep where he inexplicably slowed down on a seam route with the ball already in the air. I have no explanation for it, and I'm not going to invent one. And there are days when a corner he should bully locks him up anyway. The physical mismatch is there on every snap, but the dominance doesn't always show up with it.

That last one sounds like a high bar, but it's the knock that follows him around, and I co-sign it: given the advantage he owns on every rep, he should be more dominant at the catch point than he already is. His run blocking shows the same pattern. There's a rep of him flattening a high-profile safety, so he clearly has it in him. It just doesn't come out every week. None of this is about his talent. It's about the difference between winning most of his reps and winning every rep, and receivers built like him only become stars when they win just about every rep, because a guy who isn't separating much has to make up the difference by being ruthless at the catch point.

One last thing: he's not much of a threat after the catch, but honestly, that was never going to be his job. Almost nobody his size is a run-after-catch player, and his value was never going to live there. It's just something to know going in.

## NFL translation

True X receiver, three levels, with the intermediate middle and the red zone as his best real estate. Chain-mover on third down from day one. He's also young for the class, a true junior who'd be one of the youngest receivers in the group if he declares, which buys his consistency curve some patience.

Deployment is going to matter a lot with him: he needs a quarterback willing to throw him open and an offense that treats contested completions as a feature rather than a bailout. In that setting he starts early and produces early. Whether he gets all the way to stardom depends on the 2026 answers below.

## What I'm watching in 2026

The one that matters most to me: does he create separation that projects, or is he purely a positioning receiver? Everything else on this list comes back to that.

Press coverage is the first thing. College corners rarely bothered pressing him, the NFL will do it immediately, and his answers are close to unscouted. I also want to see the downfield drift clean up, so the big frame actually keeps the window it creates. Then there are the quiet games. Do the inexplicable locked-down afternoons disappear? That would say more about his ceiling than another 1,000 yards. And eventually, the stopwatch, because his testing number will matter more than almost anyone's in this class. At his size, a 4.55 and a 4.7 are two completely different NFL careers.

## What would change the evaluation

Every-rep catch-point violence plus cleaned-up route spacing pushes this evaluation up hard, and an early press answer would push it further. More quiet games against beatable corners push it down, because catch-point receivers don't get to have quiet games. And one verified speed number, whenever it arrives, will move the projection more than any highlight.
