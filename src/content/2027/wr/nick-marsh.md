# Nick Marsh

*Indiana, by way of Michigan State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Preseason read, subject to revision by actual football. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Nick Marsh looks like the finished product. He's 6'3" and about 215 with a dense lower body, he lines up on the line of scrimmage and absorbs contact like it's a formality, and he passes the eye-test portion of the NFL audition before the ball is snapped. Add the transfer story (Michigan State to Indiana, escaping quarterback play so poor the quarterback got benched) and the popular read assembles itself: finished body, suppressed production, wait for a real offense and enjoy the breakout.

I mostly buy the breakout. Indiana should be very good for him. But the interesting problem in this file is one the breakout narrative skates right past: Marsh has real route skill and real strength and still doesn't get open enough. That sentence should bother you. It bothers me. And in scouting, the why behind an outcome matters more than the outcome, because the why decides whether coaching can fix it. This profile is mostly an attempt to run down the why.

## The part with no argument: ball in hand

Start with what's settled, because one trait on this file is about as settled as summer scouting gets. After the catch, Marsh is a problem. He's a violent runner who sheds defensive backs the way a running back does, which makes sense, because he was one in high school. The numbers are absurd for a receiver: 28 forced missed tackles last season, with roughly half of all tackle attempts against him failing. The catch-to-run transition is instant, no gather step, already downhill. And there's real creativity mixed into the violence; he's more than a straight-line battering ram.

One note rides along: he occasionally dances. Tries to turn a seven-yard gain into a house call and turns it into four instead. That's a decision-making note, a familiar one for former high school running backs, and it's the cheapest thing on this file to fix.

File the blocking here too. At its best, his blocking is some of the best receiver blocking in this class. It just isn't every rep yet, and the gap between his best blocking rep and his average one is effort, which he controls. Strength and toughness appear nowhere on this profile as questions. Hold that thought, because it makes a later section confusing.

## The skill is real too

This matters, because the lazy version of Marsh is "power guy, needs polish," and the tape argues with the lazy version. He runs tempoed stems, meaning he changes speeds through the route's approach so the corner can't sit comfortably on the break. He sinks his hips at the top on angled routes and hard cuts alike. The head fakes and route selling are honest craft. Against true press he gets off the line cleanly, with real hand usage, and the press answers are good enough to make inside-outside versatility a live possibility.

One specific flash deserves its own paragraph: a late gear at the breakpoint. He'll run near full speed through the stem, then find an extra burst exactly at the cut, and that timing turns tight coverage into a catchable window right as the ball arrives. Breakpoint re-acceleration is a trait you associate with high-end NFL separators. It's on his tape more than once, and it's the single strongest piece of evidence for the optimistic version of this evaluation.

I'll attach my own caution to all of it: polish reads flatter a receiver when the corners are ordinary, and his route craft deserves a re-check against better coverage in 2026. But on current film, the technical case is real.

## So why isn't he open more?

Now the puzzle. The outcome itself isn't in dispute: watch him, and the space just isn't there often enough for a receiver with this skill. Separation is an outcome, though, produced by mechanisms, so let's walk the suspects.

Suspect one: the routes aren't actually as refined as they look. I can't fully dismiss it, and I flagged the corner-quality caveat above. But it's a weak lead. There's too much specific, repeatable detail on tape (the tempo, the hip work, the breakpoint gear) for "unrefined" to carry the explanation.

Suspect two: long speed. This is where the evidence keeps pointing, and it's currently my primary read. He gets caught from behind on tape. Release wins at the line don't turn into stacked corners down the field. The perimeter separation you'd expect from his skill level shows up mostly toward the middle of the field, where routes are shorter and speed is negotiable. When a corner loses early in the rep and still recovers to close the space, that's a speed statement, and corners kept making it.

Suspect three, the optimist's case: quick feet, real burst, enough juice to work vertically, with development still coming. I keep this one alive for a specific reason: nobody has a number. No verified testing exists, so every speed claim about Marsh, mine included, is an eyeball estimate. Treat "Marsh can't run" as the hypothesis with the most supporting film, and nothing stronger than that.

Why the diagnosis matters this much: coaching fixes technique. It doesn't add a gear. If the gap is technical, the breakout narrative is probably right and the ceiling is high. If the gap is athletic, his NFL game has to be built on winning through corners instead of away from them. Which brings us to the part of this file that legitimately annoys me.

## The catch point, or: the fixable thing that would change everything

A receiver who can't outrun coverage can still out-fight it at the ball. Marsh currently doesn't, and the numbers are ugly: a 27.3% contested-catch rate last season, at 6'3" and 215 pounds. For his size, that isn't a weakness. That's a malfunction.

The mechanism is visible and specific: he's a body catcher. He lets the throw arrive into his frame instead of attacking it in the air with his hands. Against tight coverage, that surrenders the whole advantage: the ball comes down into a crowd where the defender gets a full extra beat to play through it, instead of being taken above the crowd at full extension. The drop rate (9.4%, poor) comes from the same posture. Balls into the body get juggled. Balls attacked at extension get caught.

Two things make this the most interesting flaw on the file. First, nothing physical explains it. The length is there, the strength is there, and this is a player who runs into contact for fun the moment the ball is in his hands. He isn't shy. He just doesn't attack the ball. Second, that means it's behavior, and behavior can be retrained. Every part of his game says the retraining should take.

I'd still add the caution the file deserves: "coachable" is scouting's favorite word for a flaw nobody has actually fixed yet. Watch whether it changes, and don't pay for the fix until you see it.

## How the futures branch

Two questions dominate this evaluation, so the projection is a branch. If the speed is better than the film suggests and the catch point gets retrained, you're looking at a big, violent, complete boundary receiver, and the breakout narrative gets vindicated in full. If the speed is capped but he learns to attack the ball, he's a chain-moving possession X with rare after-catch power for the position: a starter who wins ugly and moves sticks. If the hands improve while the speed stays merely adequate, that possession profile still plays, tilted toward the middle of the field. And if neither improves, the profile thins to strength, blocking, and YAC on manufactured touches: an NFL contributor whose target volume never matches his build.

That's a wide range. It's supposed to be. The point of naming the branches now is knowing exactly which evidence prunes them.

## Indiana changes the sample

The 2025 context can't be ignored: Michigan State's quarterback play was inaccurate enough to get benched, and Marsh still produced 660-plus yards and six touchdowns through it. He lands in an Indiana receiver room that just sent two starters to the NFL draft and cleared out target volume, next to Charlie Becker, another receiver in this class worth reading about alongside him. Better offense, better coaching, real volume: 2026 should finally be a fair trial.

A word of caution about the breakout, though, if it comes: box scores don't measure separation. A 1,100-yard season built on scheme touches and contested volume would leave every question in this file intact. The tape will have to answer, because the stats can't.

## Biggest question entering 2026

What is his actual long speed? Everything else on the file bends around that answer, and no answer exists. He's the receiver in this class whose evaluation a single verified number would restructure the most.

What I'm watching: whether he ever simply runs past a corner, one clean vertical win with no scheme help. Whether he starts attacking the ball at full extension, and whether the contested and drop numbers move with the technique. Whether the route polish holds against the better corners on the schedule. And whether the after-catch decisions tighten up, fewer dances, more downhill.

## What would change the evaluation

A verified speed number, in either direction, moves this file more than any single game could. Visible catch-point retraining moves it almost as much, because it rescues the most likely version of his NFL role. If 2026 brings a big statistical season while the contested profile stays broken and the vertical wins stay absent, I'll take the production seriously and keep the same questions. And if he shows up attacking the ball at extension while stacking somebody deep even once a month, the ceiling case stops being a leap.

What he is today: a physically mature, legitimately skilled receiver whose outcomes lag his traits, with one explanation that coaching fixes and one it doesn't. The 2026 season gets to tell us which explanation owns the file.
