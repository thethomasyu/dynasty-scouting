# Nick Marsh

*Indiana, by way of Michigan State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Preseason read, subject to revision by actual football. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Nick Marsh looks like the finished product. He's 6'3" and about 215 with a dense lower body, he lines up on the line of scrimmage and shrugs off contact, and he passes the eye-test portion of the NFL audition before the ball is even snapped. Add the transfer story (Michigan State to Indiana, escaping quarterback play so bad the quarterback got benched) and the popular take writes itself: finished body, suppressed production, wait for a real offense and enjoy the breakout.

I mostly buy the breakout. Indiana should be very good for him. But there's a problem the breakout story skips right past, and it's the most interesting thing in his evaluation: Marsh has real route skill and real strength and still doesn't get open enough. That combination bothers me. In scouting, why something happens matters more than the fact that it happens, because that's what decides whether coaching can fix it. Most of this profile is me trying to run down that reason.

## After the catch

I'll start with what's settled, because one part of his game is about as settled as summer scouting gets. With the ball in his hands, Marsh is a problem. He's a violent runner who sheds defensive backs the way a running back does, which makes sense, because he was one in high school. The numbers are wild for a receiver: 28 forced missed tackles last season, with roughly half of all tackle attempts against him failing. The catch-to-run transition is instant. No gather step, already downhill. And there's real creativity mixed in with the violence; he's more than a straight-line battering ram.

One thing to note, though: he occasionally dances. He'll try to turn a seven-yard gain into a house call and turn it into four instead. It's a decision-making habit, a familiar one for former high school running backs, and it's the cheapest thing on this list to fix.

I'll file the blocking here too. At its best, his blocking is some of the best receiver blocking in this class. It just isn't every rep yet, and the gap between his best blocking rep and his average one is pure effort, which he controls. Strength and toughness are nowhere in this profile as questions. Keep that in mind, because it makes what happens at the catch point even more confusing. I'll get to that later.

## The skill is real too

This part matters, because the lazy take on Marsh is "power guy, needs polish," and the tape doesn't back that up. He runs tempoed stems, meaning he changes speeds through the route's approach so the corner can't sit comfortably on the break. He sinks his hips at the top of angled routes and hard cuts alike. The head fakes and route selling are honest craft. Against true press he gets off the line cleanly, with real hand usage, and the press answers are good enough that inside-outside versatility is a live possibility.

Another thing that stood out to me is the late gear at the breakpoint. He'll run near full speed through the stem, then find an extra burst exactly at the cut, and that timing turns tight coverage into a catchable window right as the ball arrives. Breakpoint re-acceleration is a trait you associate with high-end NFL separators. It's on his tape more than once, and it's the single strongest piece of evidence that the ceiling is real.

I'll attach my own caution to all of it: route polish always looks better when the corners are ordinary, and his craft deserves a re-check against better coverage in 2026. But on the current film, the skill is real.

## So why isn't he open more?

Now for the part that confuses me. Watch him and the space just isn't there often enough for a receiver with this skill. That part isn't in question. What I want to figure out is why, so let me walk through the three possible explanations.

Explanation one: the routes aren't actually as refined as they look. I can't fully dismiss it, and I flagged the corner-quality caveat above. I don't think it's the main answer, though. There's too much specific, repeatable detail on tape (the tempo, the hip work, the breakpoint gear) for "unrefined" to carry the whole answer.

Explanation two: long speed. This is where the evidence keeps pointing, and it's my primary read right now. He gets caught from behind on tape. Wins at the line don't turn into stacked corners down the field. The separation you'd expect from his skill level shows up mostly toward the middle of the field, where routes are shorter and speed is negotiable. When a corner loses early in the rep and still recovers to close the space, that tells you something about the receiver's top gear, and corners kept doing it to him.

Explanation three, the optimistic one: quick feet, real burst, enough juice to work vertically, with development still coming. I keep this one alive for a specific reason: nobody has a number. No verified testing exists, so every speed claim about Marsh, mine included, is an eyeball estimate. Treat "Marsh can't run" as the hypothesis with the most supporting film and nothing more than that.

Why does the diagnosis matter this much? Because coaching can fix technique, but it can't add a gear. If the gap is technical, the breakout story is probably right and the ceiling is high. If the gap is athletic, his NFL game has to be built on winning through corners instead of away from them. Which brings me to the part of his game that legitimately annoys me.

## The catch point

A receiver who can't outrun coverage can still out-fight it at the ball. Marsh currently doesn't, and the numbers are ugly: a 27.3% contested-catch rate last season, at 6'3" and 215 pounds. For a receiver his size, that's more than a weakness. Something specific has to be causing it.

And you can see exactly what it is on film. He's a body catcher. He lets the throw arrive into his frame instead of attacking it in the air with his hands. Against tight coverage, that gives away his entire advantage: the ball comes down into a crowd where the defender gets a full extra beat to play through it, instead of getting taken above the crowd at full extension. The drop rate (9.4%, poor) comes from the same posture. The balls he lets into his body get juggled, and the ones he attacks at extension get caught.

What makes this the most interesting flaw he has is that nothing physical explains it. The length is there, the strength is there, and this is a player who runs into contact for fun the moment the ball is in his hands. He isn't shy. He just doesn't attack the ball. And if nothing physical explains it, then it's a habit, and a habit can be retrained. Everything else about how he plays says the retraining should take.

I'll add a caution here, though. It's easy to call a flaw "coachable" before anyone has actually fixed it. Watch whether it changes, and don't pay for the fix until you see it.

## How the projection branches

Two questions dominate this evaluation, so the projection has to branch. If the speed is better than the film suggests and the catch point gets retrained, you're looking at a big, violent, complete boundary receiver, and the breakout story gets proven right in full. If the speed is capped but he learns to attack the ball, he's a chain-moving possession X with rare after-catch power for the position: a starter who wins ugly and moves the sticks. If the hands improve while the speed stays merely adequate, that possession profile still plays, tilted toward the middle of the field. And if neither improves, what's left is strength, blocking, and YAC on manufactured touches: an NFL contributor whose target volume never matches his build.

That's a wide range, but a summer profile is supposed to have one. I'm writing the branches down now so I know exactly which evidence crosses each one off.

## What Indiana changes

The 2025 context can't be ignored: Michigan State's quarterback play was inaccurate enough to get the quarterback benched, and Marsh still produced 660-plus yards and six touchdowns through it. Now he lands in an Indiana receiver room that just sent two starters to the NFL draft and cleared out target volume, next to Charlie Becker, another receiver in this class worth reading alongside him. He gets a better offense, better coaching, and real volume now, so 2026 should finally be a fair test.

One caution about the breakout, though, if it comes: box scores don't measure separation. An 1,100-yard season built on scheme touches and contested volume would leave every question here exactly where it stands.

## Biggest question entering 2026

What is his actual long speed? Everything else in this evaluation depends on that answer, and right now nobody has one. I can't think of another receiver in this class whose evaluation would change more from a single verified number.

What I'm watching: whether he ever just runs past a corner, one clean vertical win with no scheme help. Whether he starts attacking the ball at full extension, and whether the contested and drop numbers move with the technique. I also want to see the route polish hold up against the better corners on the schedule, and the after-catch decisions tighten up, fewer dances, more downhill.

## What would change the evaluation

A verified speed number, in either direction, moves this evaluation more than any single game could. Visible catch-point retraining moves it almost as much, because it rescues the role he's most likely to end up playing in the NFL. If 2026 brings a big statistical season while the contested profile stays broken and the vertical wins stay absent, I'll take the production seriously and keep the same questions. And if he shows up attacking the ball at extension while stacking somebody deep even once a month, betting on the ceiling stops being a leap.

What he is today: a physically mature, skilled receiver whose outcomes lag behind his traits, with one explanation coaching can fix and one it can't. The 2026 season gets to tell us which explanation is the right one.
