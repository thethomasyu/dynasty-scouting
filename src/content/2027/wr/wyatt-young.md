# Wyatt Young

*Oklahoma State, by way of North Texas. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. One extra caveat on this one: he may not be in this class at all.*

Wyatt Young's sophomore season was the kind that breaks stat pages at a Group of Five program: over 1,200 receiving yards in eight starts, ten touchdowns, ten catches of 40-plus yards, and the second-most receiving yards in a single season in North Texas history. Then his head coach took the Oklahoma State job, and Young followed him through the portal, scheme and all.

I'll admit this is one of my favorite evaluations in the class, and also one of the most heavily qualified. The skills that show up are loud and specific, but my questions about him are all structural: the level of competition, the stopwatch, and the calendar itself, because there's a real chance he stays in school and this whole evaluation belongs to the next class. I'll get to the skills first and the qualifiers after.

## What's real regardless of level

The burst and the release package travel together, and they're the core of him. Young reaches top speed almost immediately, and from the slot his releases produce instant short-range separation, snap after snap. The second-level work stands out just as much: he re-wins leverage against defenders lurking ten yards downfield, which is its own skill, and one most slot receivers at his level never develop. Within routes there's real wiggle, sharp cuts with burst on both sides of the break.

After the catch he's a problem. He forced 32 missed tackles last season, a number that stands with the best in this class regardless of competition level, and it's built on a sturdy lower body and real balance rather than pure elusiveness. Pair that with a zone radar, an exceptional feel for where the soft spots live, and you get his explosive-play volume. The ten 40-yard catches weren't all footraces. Plenty of them started as a free release, a found window, and a missed tackle.

The frame is 6'0" and around 200, solid enough to imagine inside-outside work. That projection is guesswork right now, though, because 93% of his snaps came from the slot, with free releases, against Group of Five coverage. It's worth keeping that in mind next to everything above it.

## The vertical question

One bad game in his season complicates the deep-ball projection. It was out of character with the rest of it, and its worst feature was tracking: several deep balls he simply never found, misses bad enough to make a fair viewer ask whether the vertical role should be his at all. First-year quarterback chemistry is real context, and one game is one game. But until the tracking question answers itself, the cautious projection keeps his work between the numbers, and the body-catching habit underneath his otherwise reliable hands points the same direction.

There's a pacing note here too: he runs everything at top speed. It's the same one-speed critique that shows up in Jeremiah Smith's profile, attached to a very different athlete. When your whole separation game is burst, giving corners one constant velocity to calibrate against works against you. Gear changes are the cheapest upgrade available to him.

## The three qualifiers

The level: everything above happened against Group of Five coverage, and 2026 moves the whole act to the Big 12. Trait for trait, this season is a validation exercise.

The stopwatch: is he fast, or game-fast? Nobody knows, and I mean that literally. No measurement exists, and the only defensible evaluation of his long speed right now is a shrug. The burst is proven. The last gear is a question that testing, or a power-conference safety's pursuit angle, will eventually answer.

The calendar: he's a true junior, and returning to school is a live possibility, arguably the likelier one. Every projection here carries that footnote.

## NFL translation

The projection, whenever it becomes relevant: an inside-out separator and chain-mover whose game is built on instant acceleration, second-level savvy, and after-catch value. That's a middle-of-the-field weapon in any modern offense. The vertical role is unproven in both directions, and we've barely seen him tested against outside press at all. If the tracking cleans up and the speed verifies, the ceiling conversation reopens. If they don't, he's an underneath weapon, and a good one.

## The audition year

What I need to see is whether the whole package survives the jump in competition with the traits intact. It's bigger than any single trait.

I'm watching the deep balls first, because the tracking question decides the shape of his role: found and finished, or middle-of-the-field for good. Then gear changes, any evidence of pacing layered on top of the burst. Then the new exams: outside snaps, press exposure, anything his college role never tested. If his power-conference season looks like the North Texas tape, most of the doubts settle at once. If the separation thins against real coverage, it recalibrates everything backward.

And the biggest change condition of all doesn't involve film: whether he declares. The dateline on this profile isn't a joke. He might belong to the next class, and until he decides, he's the best player in this class who might not be in it.
