# Wyatt Young

*Oklahoma State, by way of North Texas. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. One extra caveat on this one: he may not be in this class at all.*

Wyatt Young's sophomore season was the kind that breaks stat pages at a Group of Five program: over 1,200 receiving yards in eight starts, ten touchdowns, ten catches of 40-plus yards, the second-most receiving yards in a single season in North Texas history. Then his head coach took the Oklahoma State job, and Young followed him through the portal, scheme and all.

The file on him is one of the most enjoyable in the class and one of the most heavily qualified. The skills that show up are loud and specific. The questions are structural: the level of competition, the stopwatch, and the calendar itself, because there's a real chance he stays in school and this whole evaluation belongs to the next class. I'll take the skills first and the qualifiers after.

## What's real regardless of level

The burst and the release package travel together, and they're the core of him. Young reaches top speed almost immediately, and from the slot his releases produce instant short-range separation, snap after snap. The second-level work stands out just as much: he re-wins leverage against defenders lurking ten yards downfield, which is its own skill and one that most slot receivers at his level never develop. Within routes there's real wiggle, sharp cuts with burst on both sides of the break.

After the catch he's a problem. He forced 32 missed tackles last season, a number that stands with the best in this class regardless of competition level, and it's built on a sturdy lower body and real balance rather than pure elusiveness. Pair that with a zone radar, an exceptional feel for where the soft spots live, and you get his explosive-play volume: the ten 40-yard catches weren't all footraces. Plenty of them started as a free release, a found window, and a missed tackle.

The frame is 6'0" and around 200, solid enough to imagine inside-outside work. Imagining is all anyone can do so far: 93% of his snaps came from the slot, with free releases, against Group of Five coverage. That sentence sits under everything above it.

## The vertical question

One afternoon complicates the deep-ball projection. In his file there's one bad afternoon, out of character with the rest of his season, and its worst feature was tracking: several deep balls he simply never found, misses bad enough to make a fair viewer ask whether the vertical role should be his at all. First-year quarterback chemistry is real context, and one game is one game. Until the tracking question answers itself, though, the cautious projection keeps his work between the numbers, and the body-catching habit underneath his otherwise reliable hands points the same direction.

There's also a pacing note: he runs everything at top speed, the same one-speed critique that appears in Jeremiah Smith's file, attached here to a very different athlete. When your separation game is burst, giving corners a constant velocity to calibrate against is a self-imposed tax. Gear changes are the cheapest upgrade available to him.

## The three qualifiers

The level: everything above happened against Group of Five coverage, and 2026 moves the whole act to the Big 12. Trait for trait, this season is a validation exercise.

The stopwatch: is he fast, or game-fast? Nobody knows, and I mean that literally: no measurement exists, and the only defensible evaluation of his long speed is a shrug. Burst is proven. The last gear is a question that testing or a power-conference safety angle will eventually answer.

The calendar: he's a true junior, and returning to school is a live possibility, arguably the likelier one. Every projection here carries that footnote.

## NFL translation

The projection, wherever it lands on the calendar: an inside-out separator and chain-mover whose game is built on instant acceleration, second-level savvy, and after-catch value. That's a middle-of-the-field weapon in any modern offense. The vertical role is unproven in both directions, and the outside-press version of him is completely unwritten. If the tracking cleans up and the speed verifies, the ceiling conversation reopens; if they don't, he's an underneath weapon, and a good one.

## The audition year

The biggest question isn't a trait: it's whether the whole package survives the jump in competition with the traits intact.

I'm watching the deep balls first, because the tracking question decides his role shape: found and finished, or middle-of-field for good. Then gear changes, any evidence of pacing layered onto the burst. Then the new exams: outside snaps, press exposure, anything his college role never tested. A power-conference season that looks like the North Texas tape settles most of the doubts at once; a season where the separation thins against real coverage recalibrates everything backward.

And the biggest change condition of all is a decision, no film required: whether he declares. Until that answers itself, he's the best player in this class who might not be in it.
