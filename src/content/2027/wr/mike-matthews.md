# Mike Matthews

*Tennessee. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

No receiver in this class has a wider gap between what his routes earn and what his hands keep. Mike Matthews, a former five-star at 6'1" and 200 pounds whose breakout is running two years behind schedule, might be the best pure route-menu prospect in the middle of this class. He's also dropping and losing enough catchable balls to make the routes almost beside the point. His profile is that collision and not much else, so this one stays compact.

## The route menu

Most college receivers run one cut family well. They're hard-cut route runners or angled-route runners, and coverage figures out which. Matthews runs the full menu: deep hitches set up with a convincing vertical sell, clean deep outs, and a seam double move that might be the single prettiest route I can point to in this class's middle tier. The separation comes from the routes themselves, from footwork and selling rather than raw speed, and his athletic profile backs it capably: instant acceleration to top gear, quality movement for the size, nothing freakish.

Full-menu route running at this age is rare, and it's the specific skill NFL teams keep discovering they can't teach. That's the case for him, and it's a real one.

## The drops

The numbers against: a drop rate above fifteen percent and a contested rate below thirty. Both hold up from any angle you check, so there's no sample-size argument to hide behind. And here's the detail that actually worries me: nobody has put a clean mechanism on it. Body catching? Concentration? Timing? The record doesn't say. An undiagnosed catching problem is harder to price than a diagnosed one, because you can't tell whether you're buying a technique fix or a chronic condition.

The optimistic frame treats it as consistency and comfort, correctable with volume. The pessimistic frame says catch reliability this poor disqualifies him from the tier his routes deserve. Both fit the current record. The difference between them is patience.

## The press problem

One more oddity, and it's a strange one: the same feet that create mid-route separation get him knocked around by press. Route footwork usually transfers to the line of scrimmage, so when it doesn't, something needs explaining. Part of the explanation may be environmental, since his program's receivers carry a perennial NFL projection discount, and underdeveloped press answers are a chunk of why. Part of it is on him, because skill this obvious shouldn't stay this shy against contact.

## NFL translation

If the catching stabilizes to merely average, a full-menu separator with adequate athleticism is a starting, slot-capable NFL receiver, and the five-star pedigree stops looking like a punchline. If it doesn't, the routes stop mattering. The third-year season is the trial: watch the drop count with real volume, watch whether the press answers ever start borrowing from the route footwork, and enjoy the seam double move ruining safeties' film sessions in the meantime. A normal-drop season flips this evaluation faster than any player in the class's middle tier. Another fifteen-percent year probably ends the argument.
