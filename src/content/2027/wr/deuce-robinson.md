# Deuce Robinson

*Florida State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Deuce Robinson is 6'6" and 222 pounds, and that body comes with a job description: bully corners, own the catch point, drag people. Then you watch him, and the description is backwards. Robinson plays a finesse game. He's crafty where the frame promises crude. And for all that size, he plays soft. That gap between his body and his game shows up all over his evaluation, and it leads to a question you don't usually ask about a receiver: which position is he?

## The craft

The surprise on his film is intelligence. Robinson works to spots like a receiver who's been taught, with real finesse in how he stems his routes and arrives, and he attacks zone coverage with a clarity most jumbo receivers never develop. He knows where to be, when, and why. For a player whose recruiting profile screamed "raw tools," that's the opposite of what you'd expect. His craft is ahead of his body, not the other way around.

His catch-point life is heavy: roughly a third of his catches last season were contested, 18 won out of 38 tries on 56 total catches. Decent results, but the volume is the real finding. A receiver who lives at the catch point that much isn't choosing to. The separation isn't coming, and the 6'6" strike zone gives corners a big, legal target to play through on the way to the ball. On screens, where the geometry favors him against small defensive backs, he reliably runs through the first tackle. That's the one place where his size actually shows up as force.

## The play-strength problem

Robinson gets bumped around on routes far too easily for a man his size. Contact he should barely feel moves him off his line, and corners have figured out that the giant is the one who can be muscled. What beats him in coverage is play strength through the route, not his movement.

The blocking tells the same story: sometimes good, too often without fire. And that fire matters no matter which way he goes, because both of his realistic NFL futures demand physicality. The movement itself is closer to a tight end's, without top-end speed or receiver-grade elusiveness, and the frame could plausibly carry 235.

## The position question

Part of the industry viewed him as a tight end coming out of high school. The way he plays says receiver, but at his size he could be either. Add mass and fire and he's a matchup tight end, the flex piece who feasts on linebackers. Add play strength while holding the weight and he's a jumbo X or big slot. Either way, he has to add physicality he hasn't shown yet. Can you teach that to a player who's never had to play that way?

The pedigree says the athlete is real: five-star receiver recruit, the number-one high school baseball player in his state, and one of only three athletes ever named a football-and-baseball All-American by a major all-star organization, a distinction he shares with two current NFL stars. Elite two-sport résumés don't guarantee anything. They do lower the odds that what's missing is ability.

## NFL translation

His value depends on the team more than most: an offense hunting a jumbo mismatch piece would board him a lot higher than any general ranking implies, and a team with no such role has little use for the archetype at all. What I'm watching in 2026 is really three questions, and they all come back to physicality: does contact stop moving him, does the blocking fire show up weekly, and does any added mass arrive without dulling the movement? A stronger Robinson changes everything, because the craft is already ahead of schedule. Another finesse season at 222 would make the tight end idea the real plan, not just background talk.
