# Deuce Robinson

*Florida State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Deuce Robinson is 6'6" and 222 pounds, and that body comes with a job description: bully corners, own the catch point, drag people. Then you watch him, and the description is backwards. Robinson plays a finesse game. He's crafty where the frame promises crude, and soft in exactly the places the body promises hard. That inversion runs through his entire evaluation, and it ends at a question most receivers never face: which position is he?

## The craft

The surprise on his film is intelligence. Robinson works to spots like a receiver who's been taught, with real finesse in how he stems his routes and arrives, and he attacks zone coverage with a clarity most jumbo receivers never develop. He knows where to be, when, and why. For a player whose recruiting profile screamed "raw tools," having the craft ahead of the body is a complete reversal of the usual project.

His catch-point life is heavy: roughly a third of his catches last season were contested, 18 won out of 38 tries on 56 total catches. Decent results, but the volume is the real finding. A receiver who lives at the catch point that much isn't choosing to. The separation isn't coming, and the 6'6" strike zone gives corners a big, legal target to play through on the way to the ball. On screens, where the geometry favors him against small defensive backs, he reliably runs through the first tackle. That's the one context where the size actually cashes in as force.

## The play-strength problem

Now the cost of the inversion. Robinson gets bumped around on routes far too easily for a man his size. Contact he should barely feel moves him off his line, and corners have figured out that the giant is the one who can be muscled. The mechanism behind his coverage struggles is play strength through the route, not movement skill, and it's the single most backwards fact about him.

The blocking tells the same story: sometimes good, too often without fire. And the fire matters twice over, because both of his realistic NFL futures charge admission in physicality. The movement itself is tight-end-flavored, without top-end speed or receiver-grade elusiveness, and the frame could plausibly carry 235.

## The position question

So here's the fork. Part of the industry viewed him as a tight end coming out of high school. The play style says receiver. The body would accept either. Add mass and fire and he's a matchup tight end, the flex piece who feasts on linebackers. Add play strength while holding the weight and he's a jumbo X or big slot. Either way, the path runs through physicality he hasn't shown yet, which makes his development question unusually specific: can you coach violence into a player who has never needed any?

The pedigree says the athlete is real: five-star receiver recruit, the number-one high school baseball player in his state, and one of only three athletes ever named a football-and-baseball All-American by a major all-star organization, a distinction he shares with two current NFL stars. Elite two-sport résumés don't guarantee anything. They do lower the odds that what's missing is ability.

## NFL translation

His value depends on the team more than most: an offense hunting a jumbo mismatch piece would board him a lot higher than any general ranking implies, and a team with no such role has little use for the archetype at all. What I'm watching in 2026 is really one thing wearing three outfits: does contact stop moving him, does the blocking fire show up weekly, and does any added mass arrive without dulling the movement? A stronger Robinson changes everything, because the craft is already ahead of schedule. Another finesse season at 222 turns the tight end idea from background chatter into a plan.
