# Deuce Robinson

*Florida State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Deuce Robinson is 6'6" and 222 pounds, which comes with a job description: bully corners, own the catch point, drag people. Then you watch him, and the description is backwards. Robinson plays a finesse game, crafty where the frame promises crude, smart where you expect smash, and soft in exactly the places the body promises hard. That inversion runs the entire file, and it ends at a question most receivers never face: which position is he?

## Crafty where you expect crude

The surprise on his film is intelligence. Robinson works to spots like a receiver who's been taught, with real finesse in how he stems and arrives, and he attacks zone coverage with a clarity most jumbo receivers never develop: he knows where to be, when, and why. For a player whose recruiting profile screamed "raw tools," the craft being ahead of the body is a full reversal of the usual project.

The catch-point life is heavy: roughly a third of his catches last season were contested, 18 won out of 38 tries on 56 total catches. Decent results, and the volume is the finding. A receiver who lives at the catch point that much isn't choosing to; the separation isn't coming, and the 6'6" strike zone gives corners a big, legal target to play through on the way to the ball. On screens, where the geometry favors him against small defensive backs, he reliably runs through the first tackle. That's the one context where the size cashes as force.

## Soft where you expect hard

Now the inversion's cost. Robinson gets bumped around on routes far too easily for a man his size. Contact he should barely register moves him off his line, and corners have figured out that the giant is the one who can be muscled. Play strength through the route, not movement skill, is the flagged mechanism behind his coverage struggles, and it's the single most backwards fact on the file.

The blocking tells the same story: sometimes good, too often without fire. And the fire matters doubly for him, because both of his realistic NFL futures charge admission in physicality. The movement itself is tight-end-flavored, without top-end speed or receiver-grade elusiveness, and the frame could plausibly carry 235.

## The position question

So the fork: part of the industry viewed him as a tight end coming out of high school, the play style says receiver, and the body would accept either. Add mass and fire and he's a matchup tight end, the flex piece who feasts on linebackers. Add play strength while holding the weight and he's a jumbo X or big slot. Either way, the path runs through physicality he hasn't shown yet, which makes his development question unusually specific: can you coach violence into a game that grew up on finesse?

The pedigree says the athlete is real: five-star receiver recruit, the number-one high school baseball player in his state, and one of three athletes ever named a football-and-baseball All-American by a major all-star organization, sharing that distinction with two current NFL stars. Elite two-sport résumés don't guarantee anything, and they do lower the odds that what's missing is ability.

## Translation, sized to team

His value is team-shaped rather than linear: an offense hunting a jumbo mismatch piece would board him meaningfully higher than any general ranking implies, and a team with no such role has little use for the archetype at all. What I'm watching in 2026 is nearly all one thing wearing three outfits: does contact stop moving him, does the blocking fire show weekly, and does any added mass arrive without dulling the movement? A stronger Robinson changes everything, because the craft is already ahead of schedule. Another finesse season at 222 turns the tight end conversation from whisper into plan.
