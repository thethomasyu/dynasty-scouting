# Charlie Becker

*Indiana. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Small sample, loud numbers, handle accordingly. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

A year ago, Charlie Becker was a special teams player with a local rumor attached: around Bloomington, the line was that Indiana's third receiver was secretly its best one. Then the title run happened, the two veterans ahead of him went to the NFL draft, and the rumor went national. Now he shows up on early boards higher than almost anyone expected, and the numbers people quote about him sound made up.

Here's my angle, and it's really a warning about evidence: Becker owns some of the loudest per-target numbers in the class, produced inside the smallest job description in the class. The efficiency is real. So is the possibility that we watched a specialist doing the only two things his offense ever asked of him. This profile is mostly about learning to hold both facts at once.

## Two numbers and a caveat

The numbers first. He caught 34 passes last season at 20 yards a catch, converted contested chances at a rate in the mid-seventies, and may not have dropped a ball all year. Ten of those 34 catches went for 20-plus yards. For three months of the season he was barely used; over the championship stretch he was a weekly number-one receiver on one of the best teams in the sport.

Now the caveat, because it's structural. His route menu was verticals and red zone work, full stop, while the two NFL-bound receivers handled everything underneath. That usage funneled his targets into exactly the situations he's best at. A contested-catch rate that gaudy on that sample is elite production and a curated exam at the same time. The NFL will not curate.

## Above the rim is a skill, not a height

What makes the catch-point dominance believable rather than fluky is that the mechanism is visible. Becker is 6'3" or 6'4" depending on who's listing him, around 207, with a state-champion hurdler's résumé, and the hurdles matter: his leaping is sudden and repeatable, and it shows up directly in how he plays the ball. He climbs, times his jump off the throw rather than off the defender, and finishes through hands draped on him.

Playing above the rim is more than being tall. Tall receivers lose contested balls constantly because they arrive late, jump early, or let the corner into their chest. Becker arrives first, owns the airspace, and plucks. Add long arms and a catch radius that turns fair throws into completions, and you get a red zone weapon whose value needs no projection at all. That part of his game is NFL-ready today.

The hands underneath the highlights look clean too. The drop column from last season is either empty or close to it, and the tracking on downfield and back-shoulder throws holds up. Small sample, again. But nothing in the catching profile argues.

## How he actually gets deep

His main release is a shoulder-dip speed release, a simple tool executed cleanly, and it works because the acceleration behind it is real. Long strides eat cushion, and once he's even with a corner, the play is usually over. Man coverage in the Big Ten spent the back half of last season chasing him into the end zone.

So is he fast? This is the fork in the file, and the film supports three different answers. The championship-stretch tape says yes, plainly: he ran away from people, week after week, when everything mattered most. A harder single matchup tells a second story, where a quality corner stayed in phase and closed his separation windows late in the route. And a season-wide view lands in between: a long-strider with build-up speed who stretches the field once he's rolling rather than instantly.

I'm not going to pick a winner in July, because the samples disagree and each has a plausible excuse. What I'll say is which answer matters: if the top gear is real, the vertical dominance travels to Sundays largely as-is. If it was acceleration plus scheme leverage against college cushions, he becomes a timing-and-positioning deep threat, which is a much more quarterback-dependent life.

## The floor

Becker came up as a core special teams gunner, over 300 career snaps of it, and he plays receiver like a man who remembers that job. The blocking has real form, he's physical at the catch point without needing a coach to ask, and there's a pick-play rep on his tape delivered with genuine violence. There's football lineage behind it too: his father played at Ohio State after time in a big-league baseball organization, and his brother plays linebacker at Appalachian State.

None of that is star material. All of it is roster material, and it matters for him specifically: a vertical specialist who covers kicks and blocks is an NFL player even if the full-receiver questions come back ugly. The floor here is unusually sturdy for a one-role prospect.

## What isn't on film yet

The full route tree is a blank, and I want to be precise about what kind of blank: nobody has shown he can't run it. Indiana never asked. Route-tree breadth is a usage fact in his file, so it stays an unknown rather than a weakness.

Short-area movement is the murkier version of the same question. Can a receiver his size snap off hitches and separate underneath? The fair reads range from "the twitch is ordinary for the height" to "the change of direction is there if you dig for the reps." It stays unsettled because the offense barely ever asked him to break off a route in front of a defender.

After the catch, the file is nearly empty: he had about one meaningful run-after-catch play all season. Given the track explosiveness, I'd call that unproven rather than disproven, but as of today it's a hole. And the press picture is split by opponent quality: he powered through it against most of the schedule, and the best press corner he faced won reps against him. That one stays open.

## NFL translation

Today's version of Becker is a vertical and red zone specialist with a special teams floor, and that's a real NFL job with a long employment history. The comp people keep reaching for is Alec Pierce, and for once the lazy comp has the right shape: a long-striding vertical specialist whose game is built on stacking corners and winning the ball. Take it as a description of style and role, and stop it there.

Whether there's a complete receiver underneath the specialist is exactly what 2026 exists to answer. The two veterans are gone, the target volume is his, and Indiana now rooms him with Nick Marsh, which sets up a useful study in opposites: Marsh's file is skill outrunning outcomes, Becker's is outcomes outrunning the sample.

## The 2026 syllabus

The biggest question: was the late-season deep dominance real top-end speed, or acceleration and scheme leverage dressed up as speed? Full-tree usage answers it and three other things at once.

What I'm watching: hitches, digs, and crossers, the whole underneath menu he's never been asked to run. Whether the contested efficiency survives triple the volume against coverage that keys on him. His counters the first time a good corner takes away the speed release. And whether an after-catch game shows up now that some of his catches will finally happen in front of defenders.

What would change the evaluation: a full season at featured volume with the efficiency intact would move him up this class in a hurry, because the floor is already banked. A season of the same narrow brilliance would settle him in as a specialist, valuable and capped. And one or two clean vertical wins against the best corners on the schedule would quietly answer the speed fork, no stopwatch required.
