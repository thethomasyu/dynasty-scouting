# Charlie Becker

*Indiana. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Small sample, loud numbers, handle accordingly. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

A year ago, Charlie Becker was a special teams player with a local rumor attached: around Bloomington, the word was that Indiana's third receiver might secretly be its best one. Then the title run happened, the two veterans ahead of him left for the NFL draft, and the rumor went national. Now he shows up on early boards higher than almost anybody expected, and the numbers people quote about him sound made up.

Before anything else, I want to be upfront about the evidence: Becker owns some of the loudest per-target numbers in the class, and he produced them inside the smallest job description in the class. The efficiency is real. It's also very possible that we spent a title run watching a specialist do the only two things his offense ever asked him to do. Keep both of those in mind through the rest of this profile.

## The numbers first

He caught 34 passes last season at 20 yards a catch, converted his contested chances at a rate in the mid-seventies, and may not have dropped a single ball all year. Ten of those 34 catches went for 20-plus yards. For three months he was barely used; over the championship stretch he was a weekly number-one receiver on one of the best teams in the sport.

Now for the caveat, because it's built into how Indiana used him. His route menu was verticals and red zone work, nothing else, while the two NFL-bound veterans handled everything underneath. That usage funneled his targets into exactly the situations he's best at. So a contested-catch rate that gaudy on that sample is elite production, but it's also a test that was built in his favor, and he won't get that setup in the NFL.

## At the catch point

What makes the catch-point dominance believable instead of fluky is that you can see how he does it. Becker is 6'3" or 6'4" depending on who's listing him, around 207, with a state-champion hurdler's résumé. The hurdles matter: his leaping is sudden and repeatable, and it shows up directly in how he plays the ball. He climbs, he times his jump off the throw rather than off the defender, and he finishes with hands draped all over him.

Playing above the rim is more than just being tall. Tall receivers lose contested balls constantly, because they arrive late, jump early, or let the corner into their chest. Becker doesn't have that problem: he gets there first, owns the airspace, and plucks the ball. Add long arms and a catch radius that turns fair throws into completions, and you get a red zone weapon that needs no projection at all. That part of his game is NFL-ready today.

The hands underneath the highlights look clean too. The drop column from last season is either empty or close to it, and the tracking on downfield and back-shoulder throws holds up. Small sample, again. But nothing in the catching profile worries me.

## How he actually gets deep

His main release is a shoulder-dip speed release, a simple tool executed cleanly, and it works because the acceleration behind it is real. Long strides eat cushion in a hurry, and once he's even with a corner, the play is usually over. Man coverage in the Big Ten spent the back half of last season chasing him into the end zone.

So is he fast? This is the big argument with Becker, because the film gives you a different answer depending on which games you watch. The championship-stretch tape says yes: he ran away from people, week after week, when everything mattered most. Then there's the one harder matchup, where a quality corner stayed in phase with him and closed the separation windows late in the route. And if you watch the season as a whole, you land somewhere in between: a long strider with build-up speed who stretches the field once he gets rolling, not instantly.

I'm not picking a winner in July, because the samples disagree and each one comes with a plausible excuse. But the answer matters a lot for how you project him. If the top gear is real, the vertical dominance travels to Sundays mostly as-is. If it was acceleration plus scheme leverage against college cushions, he becomes a timing-and-positioning deep threat, which is a much more quarterback-dependent way to make a living.

## The floor

Becker came up as a core special teams gunner, over 300 career snaps of it, and he plays receiver like a guy who remembers that job. The blocking has real form, he's physical at the catch point without a coach needing to ask, and there's a pick-play rep on his tape delivered with real violence. The football lineage fits the picture: his father played at Ohio State after time in a big-league baseball organization, and his brother plays linebacker at Appalachian State.

None of that is star material, but it's the kind of stuff that keeps you on a roster, and for him specifically it matters: a vertical specialist who covers kicks and blocks is an NFL player even if the full-receiver questions come back ugly. For a one-role prospect, that's an unusually sturdy floor.

## What isn't on film yet

The full route tree is a blank, and I want to be clear about what that means: nobody has shown he can't run it. Indiana just never asked him to. Route-tree breadth is a fact about his usage, not about him, so it stays an unknown rather than a weakness.

Short-area movement is the murkier version of the same question. Can a receiver his size snap off hitches and separate underneath? You could watch him and come away thinking the twitch is ordinary for the height, but there are also reps where the change of direction is there if you dig for them. It stays unsettled because the offense barely ever asked him to break off a route in front of a defender.

After the catch, there's almost nothing: about one meaningful run-after-catch play all season. Given the track explosiveness, I'm not willing to say he can't do it, just that he hasn't shown it, but as of today it's a hole. And the press picture splits by opponent quality: he powered through it against most of the schedule, and the best press corner he faced won their matchups. Something to keep an eye on this season.

## NFL translation

Today's version of Becker is a vertical and red zone specialist with a special teams floor, and that's a real NFL job with a long employment history. The comp people keep reaching for is Alec Pierce, and for once the popular comp has the right shape: a long-striding vertical specialist whose game is built on stacking corners and winning the ball. Take it as a description of style and role, and stop there.

Whether there's a complete receiver underneath the specialist is what I want the 2026 season to answer. The two veterans are gone, the target volume is his, and Indiana now rooms him with Nick Marsh, which should be a fun one to follow because the two evaluations are basically opposites. Marsh has the skill and is still waiting on the outcomes. Becker has the outcomes, and I'm waiting on a real sample.

## What I'm watching in 2026

The biggest question: was the late-season deep dominance real top-end speed, or acceleration and scheme leverage dressed up as speed? Full-tree usage answers it and a few other questions at the same time.

So I'm watching the underneath menu he's never been asked to run: hitches, digs, crossers. I want to see whether the contested efficiency survives triple the volume against coverage that keys on him, and what his counters look like the first time a good corner takes away the speed release. I'm also curious whether an after-catch game shows up now that some of his catches will finally happen in front of defenders.

What would change the evaluation: a full season at featured volume with the efficiency intact moves him up this class in a hurry, because the floor is already banked. A season of the same narrow role, even a brilliant one, settles him in as a specialist, which is still valuable but capped. And one or two clean vertical wins against the best corners on the schedule would answer the speed question all by themselves.
