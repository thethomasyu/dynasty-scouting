# Devin McCuin

*Ohio State, by way of UTSA. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his final college season. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Ohio State doesn't import receivers. The best receiver program in the sport grows its own, which is what makes Devin McCuin interesting before a single route gets run: this offseason, that program made an exception for a Group of Five slot with the most reliable hands in the transfer market. He arrives with spring-practice buzz already attached, and with one of the cleanest natural experiments in the class waiting for him. It's the same player going to an elite program against real coverage for one season, and almost every open question about him gets answered by how that season goes.

## The hands

The signature trait needs no translation. McCuin's drop rate has lived under five percent for his whole career, and the more generous counts put it near two. He's a natural catcher, the kind whose hands work the same on the move, above his frame, and at the sideline, where the toe-tap awareness is already on tape. The contested work reads reliable too, though nobody has real numbers on it yet. A lot of the premium prospects in this class keep dropping passes, so a hands-first receiver almost stands out just for that.

## The twitch, and some it-factor

The athletic profile starts with track-background acceleration and real twitch: instant stop-start, sharp horizontal cuts with burst attached. And the route tape shows something beyond quickness, a manipulation instinct that's hard to teach. On one rep he bent a speed out and accelerated out of what looked like a dead stop. On another, he stretched a corner route and finished with a late hip flip for a walk-in touchdown. He even turned a fake block into a score by freezing the defender. He sets defenders up, and that kind of instinct usually carries from one level to the next.

The caveat on all of that: it happened against American Conference coverage. We haven't seen him do any of it against real corners yet, and that's what the season ahead is for.

## The four exams

Press: he was rarely pressed in his college role, and when it happened, his habit was to run around the contact rather than through it, which stretches routes out and breaks their timing. Running around it works fine against off coverage, but it won't hold up against NFL-caliber corners. The Big Ten will press him in September, repeatedly, and this is the exam that matters most.

Arm length: an eyeball estimate puts his arms near the threshold a lot of teams use as an outside cutoff. That's a guess, and I'm treating it as exactly that until somebody measures him. But the answer matters a lot for his projection. If his arms are long enough to play outside, that lifts his value. If he's slot-only, it caps him. Film can't settle this one; he'd have to get measured first.

Top speed: the twitch is proven, but the deep gear hasn't yet shown up as a downfield-separation weapon. Is he fast enough to threaten over the top, or just quick and reliable without that extra gear? I don't know yet.

Blocking: the reports on him flatly contradict each other, feisty and aggressive in one, anonymous in another. That could be different weeks, different standards, or real inconsistency. I don't have a verdict and won't fake one. It's the easiest of the four exams to pass, which is why it'd be a red flag if he didn't.

## NFL translation

Best case, he's a do-everything slot with more juice than that label usually implies: he lines up everywhere, he's reliable on money downs, quarterbacks trust his hands, and his manipulation game gets him open on schedule. That guy starts in the league for a decade. Worst case, he's slot-only, and that gets decided by his arms and the press answers rather than by his skill. Either way, the hands keep his floor high, because guys who catch everything find work.

## One season, four answers

The 2026 question is really several questions rolled into one: does the whole game hold up? Press, deep speed, the blocking question, and the jump in competition all get tested by the same schedule, and there's a tape measure waiting at the end for the arms.

Watch the press reps above everything. If he starts working through contact instead of around it, against that conference, the projection unlocks and the spring buzz becomes a draft-season story. If the avoidance habit survives intact, the slot ceiling hardens no matter how many balls he catches. The hands are the constant either way. The season decides what kind of receiver they belong to.
