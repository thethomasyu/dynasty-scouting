# Ryan Wingo

*Texas. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. A tools bet, and priced like one. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Ryan Wingo ran a 10.55-second 100 meters in high school, with multiple 200s under 21.5, and he did it in a body that now carries somewhere between 210 and 214 pounds at 6'2". Those numbers are the beginning of every conversation about him, and they should be. Sprinter speed usually comes in sprinter frames. Wingo's comes with a build that lets him bully corners at the top of routes, and he just put up an 834-yard, second-team All-SEC season while still visibly learning the position.

That last part is the evaluation. Wingo is the class's purest bet on tools, and what makes his file unusual is how little ambiguity there is on either side of it. What's special about him is emphatically special. What's missing is emphatically missing. There's no mystery here, just a price argument.

## What 10.55 means at this size

Track times don't catch passes, so translate it into football terms. His acceleration arrives while corners are still opening their hips, which means cushion disappears on a schedule they've never practiced against. His long speed is true home-run gear, so a single missed jam or blown leverage becomes seven points. And because 210-plus pounds arrives with the speed, his vertical stem is a physical event: he leans into corners mid-route and bursts off the contact, and defenders have to honor both the collision and the sprint at once.

One wrinkle rides with the power game: it's blatant. The stem bullying works in college partly because college officials let it go, and NFL crews will throw on some of those reps. The strength is real and the application needs a legal version. That's a real development item, and a fixable one.

The movement footnote: the athleticism runs more linear than lateral. There's a little stiffness in him, and the wiggle you'd want in a shifty separator isn't really his game. He's a runaway train, not a joystick.

## The catching problem

Now the gate, and it's the least glamorous gate in scouting: he doesn't catch the ball well enough. The drop rate ran double digits last season, the misses include wide-open, uncontested throws, and his contested rate sat under 30%, which is indefensible for a receiver built like this.

The mechanism makes it more concerning than a focus blip. He's a body catcher. Throws arrive into his frame instead of being attacked in the air, and body catching taxes everything: routine balls get juggled, contested balls give the defender a free extra beat to play through the catch, and the whole 210-pound advantage evaporates at the exact moment it should cash. This isn't a concentration story you wait out. It's a technique rebuild, hands and posture, the kind position coaches sell careers on.

Fix it and the rest of the file starts reading like a top-of-class receiver. That's how loud the tools are, and it's why he's the easiest player in the class to talk yourself into and out of on alternating days.

## The feel gap

The second gap is quieter and might matter more. Wingo runs his routes at one speed, and the speed is a great one, but coverage isn't confused by it. He shows little sense yet for space and timing: he'll run past open grass, carry routes into defenders, and give his quarterback few friendly windows against zone. The route fakes are inconsistent rather than absent; there are reps where a stem move holds a defender, and reps where he telegraphs the break badly enough that the corner beats him to it.

Feel is the trait that historically decides whether this archetype converts. The league is littered with sprinters who never learned where the soft spots live, and it employs a small, rich club of the ones who did. Nothing in Wingo's file says which club he joins. Everything in it says 2026 starts answering.

The comparison worth making is inside this class: he and Jeremiah Smith get paired as the group's overwhelming athletes, and the pairing is useful only if you keep the scale honest. Smith's gap is fine-grain detail on top of an already functional route game. Wingo's gaps start earlier, at the catch point and the feel for space. Same species of bet, very different odds.

## After the catch, with a caveat

Texas fed him manufactured touches, sweeps and screens and rushes, because size plus that speed in space is a fair fight for nobody. He forced 12 missed tackles and produced real chunk plays that way, and defenders bounce off him when the angles favor him.

The caveat is contact balance. On some touches he runs through people; on others, a squared-up tackler drops him harder than the frame suggests he should go down. For a player whose current usage is built on after-catch work, that split matters, and I'm treating it as unsettled: the burst and mass are proven, the balance through real contact is game-to-game.

## NFL translation

Day one, he's a vertical stressor and a manufactured-touch weapon, and that package alone gets a roster spot and a role: go routes, sweeps, shot plays, the gravity that makes safeties honest. The full-tree X projection exists only on the other side of the hands rebuild, and the feel question decides whether he ever becomes more than a straight-line problem.

So the projection forks the way tools bets always do. The version where the catching stabilizes and even average feel arrives is a high-end NFL starter, because nobody can match the frame-speed combination. The version where neither arrives is a decade of coaches getting fired for believing they'd be the ones to fix it.

## What 2026 has to show

The question at the top: does he attack the ball? Everything else on the file is negotiable; the catching is not.

Watching, in order: hand technique on routine throws, because the fix shows up there before the stat line moves. The contested rate, which should embarrass a 214-pound sprinter into the fifties at minimum. Any evidence of throttle, one route a game where he changes speed into a window on purpose. And the stem power played legally, wins without the push-off tax. The excuses are gone, too: Texas has real quarterback play now and Cam Coleman across the field pulling coverage, which is the friendliest possible environment for a breakout.

What moves this file: visible catch-technique change moves it up fast, faster than production would, because the mechanism is the whole gate. Another double-digit drop season at this usage makes the tools conversation academic and the projection a special-teams-adjacent gadget role. And a season of real zone feel would be the bigger surprise, the one that changes what archetype he even belongs to.
