# Ryan Wingo

*Texas. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. A tools bet, priced like one. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Ryan Wingo ran a 10.55-second 100 meters in high school, with multiple 200s under 21.5, and he did it in a body that now carries somewhere between 210 and 214 pounds at 6'2". Those numbers are the start of every conversation about him, and they should be. Sprinter speed usually comes in sprinter frames, but Wingo's comes with a build that lets him bully corners at the top of routes, and he just put up an 834-yard, second-team All-SEC season while still visibly learning how to play receiver.

That last part is honestly the whole evaluation. Wingo is the purest tools bet in the class, and what makes him unusual is how little ambiguity there is on either side of the bet. What's special about him is obvious, and what's missing is just as obvious. The real argument is about the price.

## What 10.55 means at this size

So what does that track time actually mean on a football field? Let me try to translate it. His acceleration arrives while corners are still opening their hips, which means the cushion disappears on a schedule they've never practiced against. His long speed is true home-run gear, so one missed jam or one blown leverage becomes seven points. And because 210-plus pounds arrives with the speed, his vertical stem is a physical event: he leans into corners mid-route and bursts off the contact, and defenders have to honor the collision and the sprint at the same time.

One thing to note about the power game is how blatant it is. The stem bullying works in college partly because college officials let it go, and NFL crews will throw flags on some of those reps. The strength is real. He'll need to learn a legal version of it, which is a real development item, but I think it's a fixable one.

One more thing on the movement: the athleticism runs more linear than lateral. There's a little stiffness in him, and the wiggle you'd want in a shifty separator isn't really his game. He's a runaway train, not a joystick.

## The catching problem

The biggest problem with him is that he doesn't catch the ball well enough. His drop rate ran double digits last season, the misses include wide-open, uncontested throws, and his contested rate sat under 30%, which I can't defend for a receiver built like this.

What makes it more concerning than a focus blip is how the misses happen. He's a body catcher. Throws arrive into his frame instead of getting attacked in the air, and body catching taxes everything: routine balls get juggled, contested balls give the defender a free extra beat to play through the catch, and the whole 210-pound advantage evaporates at the exact moment it should pay off. I don't think this is a concentration problem you can wait out. It's a technique rebuild, hands and posture, the kind of fix position coaches stake careers on.

If he fixes it, the rest of his game starts reading like a top-of-class receiver. The tools really are that loud, and it's why I've talked myself into and out of him more than any other player in the class.

## The feel for space

The second gap doesn't jump out the way the drops do, but it might matter more. Wingo runs his routes at one speed. It's a great speed, but coverage isn't confused by it. He shows little sense yet for space and timing: he'll run past open grass, carry routes into defenders, and give his quarterback few friendly windows against zone. The route fakes are inconsistent rather than absent. There are reps where a stem move holds a defender, and there are reps where he telegraphs the break badly enough that the corner beats him to the spot.

Feel is usually the trait that decides whether this type of receiver makes it. The league is littered with sprinters who never learned where the soft spots live, and the small group that did learn got very rich. I can't tell from the film yet which way Wingo goes, and the 2026 season should start answering that.

The comparison I keep coming back to is inside this class. He and Jeremiah Smith get paired as the group's overwhelming athletes, and the pairing only works if you're honest about how far apart they are. Smith's gap is fine-grain detail on top of an already functional route game. Wingo's gaps start earlier, at the catch point and the feel for space. They're the same kind of bet, but the odds are a lot different.

## After the catch, with a caveat

Texas fed him manufactured touches, sweeps and screens and handoffs, because size plus that speed in space is a fair fight for nobody. He forced 12 missed tackles, produced real chunk plays that way, and defenders bounce off him when the angles favor him.

The caveat is contact balance. On some touches he runs through people, and on others a squared-up tackler drops him harder than the frame says he should go down. For a player whose current usage is built on after-catch work, that split matters, and I'm treating it as unsettled: the burst and the mass are proven, and the balance through real contact is game-to-game.

## NFL translation

Day one, he's a vertical stressor and a manufactured-touch weapon, and that package alone gets a roster spot and a role: go routes, sweeps, shot plays, the gravity that keeps safeties honest. The full-tree X projection only exists on the other side of the hands rebuild, and the feel question decides whether he ever becomes more than a straight-line problem.

So the projection has a wide range, like every tools bet does. If the catching stabilizes and even average feel arrives, he's a high-end NFL starter, because nobody can match the frame-speed combination. If neither arrives, he's the type of player who gets coaches fired for a decade, because somebody will always believe they're the one who can fix him.

## What 2026 has to show

The big question for me is simple: does he attack the ball? Everything else about him is negotiable, but the catching is not.

What I'm watching, in order: hand technique on routine throws, because the fix shows up there before the stat line moves. The contested rate, which should embarrass a 214-pound sprinter into at least the fifties. Any evidence of throttle, even one route a game where he changes speed into a window on purpose. And the stem power played legally, wins without the push-off. The excuses are gone, too: Texas has real quarterback play now and Cam Coleman across the field pulling coverage, which is about the friendliest environment a breakout could ask for.

What would move the evaluation: visible catch-technique change moves it up faster than production would, because the technique is the whole problem. Another double-digit drop season at this usage makes the tools conversation academic and the projection a gadget role with special teams attached. And a season of real zone feel would be the bigger surprise, because that would change what kind of receiver we're even talking about.
