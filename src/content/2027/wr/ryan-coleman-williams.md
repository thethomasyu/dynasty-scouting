# Ryan Coleman Williams

*Alabama. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Two seasons of evidence, pointing opposite directions. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Two years ago, Ryan Coleman Williams (then just Ryan Williams; he added the Coleman this offseason) started all thirteen games at Alabama at seventeen years old, put up nearly 900 yards at 18 a catch, made first-team All-SEC, and was briefly the most famous young player in the sport. Last year he dropped his way out of the featured role, watched his snaps shrink situationally, and finished as the biggest cautionary tale of the season.

So the file contains two players wearing the same number, and the entire discipline of evaluating him is refusing to average them. A freshman season that good at that age is nearly impossible to fake. A sophomore season that broken is impossible to ignore. Both are real, and how you weigh them says more about your evidence philosophy than about him. My approach here is to sort what broke from what didn't, because they aren't the same kind of thing.

## What 2025 couldn't touch

Start with the part of his game that survived, because even the harshest cut of his film supports it. The movement is elite. Great top speed, silky change of direction, stop-start quickness, all of it live against SEC corners since he was seventeen. And it produces a specific, watchable effect that almost nobody else in this class produces: corners abandon their technique against him. They bail out of their pedal and turn to run earlier in the route than they do against anyone else, because they've done the math on what happens if they don't.

That's what receiver gravity looks like before the ball is even thrown. He stretches defenses horizontally and vertically, his open-field game is elusiveness rather than power (28 forced missed tackles across two seasons at 182 pounds is slipperiness, no other explanation), and at his best he's a downfield playmaker whose catches flip games. This is why nobody can quit him. It's also why the rest of this profile matters so much.

## The drops, precisely

The 2025 numbers are ugly from any angle: double-digit drops on around 80 targets, and a career count already in the high teens. Every path back to the top of this class runs through that column.

The mechanism matters more than the count, though, and the mechanism is strange. He makes spectacular catches, full-extension grabs, including a catch in traffic through a big hit at the exact moment the horrible-hands narrative was peaking. Then he drops routine, easy chances with nobody near him. Great difficult catches plus inexplicable easy drops points at concentration, at focus arriving and leaving, and away from a hand-talent problem.

That's the optimistic diagnosis, because concentration has a far better repair history than bad hands. It's also the diagnosis that puts everything on his habits, which is uncomfortable, because habits are exactly where the harder questions about his 2025 live.

## The physics problem

He's listed around 6'0" or 6'1" and 182 pounds, and the 182 shows up on film exactly where you'd expect: contact disrupts him, congested areas swallow him, and middle-of-field hits at that mass occasionally sent him flying. Some of the finishing failures are just physics.

Separate two things here, because the film does. Courage is not the issue. He goes into punishing areas without hesitation, throws himself at defenders, and once saved a touchdown with a legitimate hit after an interception. The mindset writes checks; the body can't always cash them. A fear problem would be close to unfixable. A mass problem, on a frame this young, is a weight room project, and there are offseason reports of added muscle that I'll believe when the contact tape shows it.

## The harder read

There's a version of the 2025 story that goes past the hands, and I'd be hiding the ball if I left it out. On some film, his route speed told you whether the play was coming to him. Late in the year, Alabama started removing him from the field on run-blocking downs and some obvious passing situations, which reads like a staff that had stopped trusting him with the full job.

I'm holding that read as a hypothesis, deliberately. Effort patterns observed across one miserable season can mislead, and a nineteen-year-old having a visible sulk during a collapsing year is not a scouting death sentence. But I flag it specifically because it's the one item on this file that a normal statistical bounce-back wouldn't disprove. A thousand yards with clean hands would answer the drops. Only the boring downs answer this.

## NFL translation

The fork is wide and honest. If the freshman version returns, he projects as a featured outside receiver whose speed bends coverage and whose route feel was already advanced for his age, a three-level starter with true number-one upside. If the 2025 version persists, the projection collapses toward a situational vertical weapon, the boom-only deep threat whose drops and blocking keep him off the field on schedule downs, and whose snap count caps everything else.

The tiebreaker nobody should skip: he plays this college season at nineteen. Even a partial rebound would make him one of the youngest premium receivers available in any class, with two full SEC seasons already banked. Age doesn't catch drops. It does buy development time that most prospects never get.

## The rebound checklist

The question over everything: which season is the player? 2026 gets to answer it almost line by line.

I'm watching four things. Whether he's on the field for all four downs by October, because snap patterns are the staff telling you what the drills look like. The drop count, obviously, and specifically the easy ones. Whether the reported muscle shows up as finishing through contact instead of finishing on the ground. And the boring reps: route speed on plays designed for other people, blocking when asked.

A clean-hands season at full usage removes the gate entirely, and this profile reads very differently next summer. Another year of the 2025 pattern makes the freshman season the outlier instead of the baseline, and that's a much cheaper player. And if the engagement questions resurface inside a healthy, featured role, that becomes the file's headline, because talent has never been the thing at risk here.
