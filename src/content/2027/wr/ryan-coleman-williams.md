# Ryan Coleman Williams

*Alabama. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Two seasons of evidence that point in opposite directions. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Two years ago, Ryan Coleman Williams (then just Ryan Williams; he added the Coleman this offseason) started all thirteen games at Alabama as a seventeen-year-old, put up nearly 900 yards at 18 a catch, made first-team All-SEC, and was briefly the most famous young player in the sport. Last year he dropped his way out of the featured role, watched his snaps shrink in specific situations, and finished the season as its biggest cautionary tale.

So you're really looking at two different players here, and I don't think averaging the two seasons together tells you much. A freshman season that good at that age is nearly impossible to fake. The sophomore year was bad enough that I can't just wave it off either. Both of them really happened. What I can try to do is figure out what actually broke and what didn't, because I don't think they're the same kind of problem.

## What survived 2025

Start with the part of his game that even the harshest cut of the film supports: the movement is elite. Great top speed, silky change of direction, stop-start quickness, and all of it live against SEC corners since he was seventeen. It produces a specific effect on film that almost nobody else in this class produces: corners abandon their technique against him. They bail out of their pedal and turn to run earlier in the route than they do against anyone else, because they've done the math on what happens if they don't.

He's bending the coverage before the ball is even in the air. He stretches defenses horizontally and vertically. His open-field game is pure elusiveness rather than power, and it works: 28 forced missed tackles across two seasons at 182 pounds doesn't happen any other way. At his best he's a downfield playmaker whose catches flip games, and that's why nobody can quit him.

## The drops

The 2025 numbers are ugly from any angle: double-digit drops on around 80 targets, and a career count already in the high teens. He's not getting back to the top of this class until that number comes down.

What matters more to me than the count is how he's dropping them, and it's strange. He makes spectacular catches, full-extension grabs, including a catch in traffic through a big hit at the exact moment the horrible-hands storyline was peaking. Then he drops routine, easy chances with nobody near him. That combination looks to me like a concentration problem, his focus coming and going, not bad hands.

Concentration has a much better repair history than bad hands do. The catch is that it puts everything on his habits, and habits are where the harder questions about his 2025 start.

## The 182-pound problem

He's listed around 6'0" or 6'1" and 182 pounds, and the 182 shows up on film exactly where you'd expect. Contact disrupts him. Congested areas swallow him. Middle-of-field hits at that weight occasionally sent him flying. Some of the finishing failures are just physics.

The film is clear that courage is not the issue. He goes into punishing areas without hesitation, throws himself at defenders, and once saved a touchdown with a legitimate hit after an interception. The mindset writes checks the body can't always cash. And that matters for how you project him, because a fear problem is close to unfixable, while extra weight on a frame this young is just a weight-room project. There are offseason reports of added muscle. I'll believe them when the contact tape shows it.

## The harder read

There's a part of the 2025 story that goes past the hands, and I'd be hiding the ball if I left it out. On some film, you could tell from his route speed whether the play was coming to him. Late in the year, Alabama started taking him off the field on run-blocking downs and some obvious passing situations, which reads like a staff that had stopped trusting him with the full job.

I'm holding that read as a hypothesis, on purpose. Effort patterns observed across one miserable season can mislead, and a nineteen-year-old having a visible sulk during a collapsing year is not a scouting death sentence. But I flag it specifically because it's the one item here that a normal statistical bounce-back wouldn't disprove. A thousand yards with clean hands would answer the drops. This one you only see on the boring downs.

## NFL translation

The range here is wide, and that's just where the evidence leaves it. If the freshman version returns, he projects as a featured outside receiver whose speed bends coverage and whose route feel was already advanced for his age, a three-level starter with true number-one upside. If the 2025 version persists, the projection collapses toward a situational vertical weapon, the boom-only deep threat whose drops and blocking keep him off the field on schedule downs, and whose snap count caps everything else.

The other thing I keep coming back to is his age. He plays this college season at nineteen. Even a partial rebound would make him one of the youngest premium receivers available in any class, with two full SEC seasons already banked. Age isn't going to catch the ball for him, but it buys development time most prospects never get.

## The rebound checklist

So which season is the real player? 2026 is going to answer that almost line by line.

I'm watching four things. Whether he's on the field for all four downs by October, because snap patterns are the staff telling you what the practices look like. The drop count, obviously, and specifically the easy ones. Whether the reported muscle shows up as finishing through contact instead of finishing on the ground. And the boring reps: route speed on plays designed for other people, blocking when he's asked.

A clean-hands season at full usage removes the gate entirely, and this profile reads very differently next summer. Another year of the 2025 pattern makes the freshman season the outlier instead of the baseline, and that's a much cheaper player. And if the engagement questions resurface inside a healthy, featured role, that's the part I'd worry about, because talent was never what was in doubt with him.
