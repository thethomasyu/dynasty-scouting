# TJ Moore

*Clemson. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. A tidy evaluation with one big variable. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Most of this class is athletes learning to play receiver. TJ Moore is the opposite: a receiver waiting on his body. He's 6'3" and somewhere around 200 pounds, he's 20 years old, and his technical game is further along than his tools, which is the rarest direction for that gap to run in a class this athletic. It also makes him unusually easy to evaluate consistently. The strengths, the flaw, and the fix all point at the same place, and the read on him barely moves no matter which angle you come at it from. In a class full of wild ranking swings, that steadiness is worth something.

## Craft first

The press work is the headline. Moore plays the line of scrimmage with educated hands. He sets corners up, breaks their punch away before it lands, and wins with a plan instead of a burst. Most college receivers with his frame just speed-release off everything and end up learning hand combat at 24, in the pros, expensively. Moore is doing it now, and the footwork matches: clean mandatory inside and outside releases, plus a two-step diamond release (the one where your first two steps threaten both edges) with instant separation attached.

The mid-route stuff holds up too. At 6'3", he can sink his hips and flip them at the top of comeback-family routes, which long-legged receivers mostly can't do; height means long levers, and long levers usually mean rounded breaks. He holds his speed through bending in-breakers instead of gearing down, which is exactly how a non-burner stays on schedule. And he understands where the soft spots in zone live and settles into them. None of it is creative or flashy, and that's a fair note on him: the routes are workmanlike, executed rather than invented. But execution at this level, at this age, is a professional foundation.

## Great hands, bad contested numbers

Now for the two facts about him that don't seem to fit together, and a small scouting lesson along the way. Moore has great hands, and Moore loses at the catch point constantly. Both are true, and his game only makes sense once you split those apart.

The hands themselves are clean: full extension, attacking the ball away from his frame, reliable in space, with some spectacular reach catches on tape. The contested record is grim: six of eighteen last season, which at 6'3" looks like a typo. The bridge between those two facts is play strength. Coverage bumps Moore off his spots before the ball ever arrives. The catch never gets to be about his hands, because the positioning battle was already lost a beat earlier, through contact he should be too big to feel this much. Catching the ball and holding your ground are different skills. He has the first one and not the second.

That diagnosis is why I stay warm on him despite the ugly contested number. A hands problem at 20 is scary. A strength problem at 20, on a 6'3" frame that's been listed as low as 195, is a nutrition plan and two offseasons. The frame has obvious room, and every failure he has routes back to the same missing pounds.

## The athletic ceiling

Now the part nobody can coach: he's not a special athlete. Nothing on his film argues otherwise, and I won't either. The speed is build-up, the burst is functional, and his long legs make the top gear arrive late rather than never. None of that stops a technician from starting NFL games. It does cap the difference-maker conversation until he proves otherwise.

There's a real fork in how to read his trajectory, and it's worth being upfront about. One fair reading says he's close to a finished product, a known quantity whose curve has already flattened. The other says a 20-year-old with this much craft and this much frame room is exactly the profile that jumps a tier when the mass arrives. I lean toward the second, because betting against players who are already good at the hard parts is historically bad business. But it's a lean, and this season tests it directly.

Some context for the numbers, whenever you look them up: he was the second option in his own offense behind Bryant Wesco Jr., which capped his target quality. And his blocking effort and fundamentals are already fine. The physicality complaint is about his own routes, never his willingness.

## NFL translation

The projection is a starter-caliber outside receiver whose margin is craft: releases, spacing, timing, catching in space. The versatile alignment history helps the case; he's played outside, inside, on and off the line. The realistic early-NFL role is a trusted second or third receiver who wins on schedule, and the ceiling question is athletic, unresolved, and easy to state: even with the mass, is there enough juice for more than that? Nobody knows yet, including him.

## What I'm watching in 2026

The season's question: does added mass convert the contested losses into wins, or is the play-strength issue something stranger than pounds?

I'm watching the scale first, whatever number Clemson lists him at in August, and then the collision reps: does contact still move him off his stem, and do the contested balls start splitting even? Second, the press craft against the best corners on the schedule, because that skill is his NFL meal ticket and it should show up weekly. And any verified athletic testing, whenever it comes, matters extra in his case: it either confirms the ceiling or deletes it.

A visibly stronger Moore winning half his contested chances moves him up a tier, because the craft is already there waiting. The same losses at the same weight would confirm the ceiling and shift his projection toward a complementary role for good. And a surprise in the speed department, in either direction, rewrites the one sentence in this profile I'm not sure about.
