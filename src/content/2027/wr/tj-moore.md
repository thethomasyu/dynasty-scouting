# TJ Moore

*Clemson. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. A tidy evaluation with one big variable. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Most of this class is athletes learning to play receiver. TJ Moore is the opposite; he already knows how to play receiver, and he's waiting on his body to catch up. He's 6'3" and somewhere around 200 pounds, he's 20 years old, and his technical game is further along than his tools, which is the rarest direction for that gap to run in a class this athletic. The strengths, the flaw, and the fix all point at the same place, and the read on him barely moves no matter which angle you come at it from.

## Craft first

The first thing that stands out is the press work. Moore plays the line of scrimmage with educated hands. He sets corners up, breaks their punch away before it lands, and wins with a plan instead of a burst. Most college receivers with his frame just speed-release off everything and end up learning hand combat at 24, in the pros, which is the expensive way to learn it. Moore is doing it now, and the footwork matches: clean mandatory inside and outside releases, plus a two-step diamond release (the one where your first two steps threaten both edges) with instant separation attached.

The mid-route stuff holds up too. At 6'3", he can sink his hips and flip them at the top of comeback-family routes, which long-legged receivers mostly can't do; height means long levers, which usually mean rounded breaks. He holds his speed through bending in-breakers instead of gearing down, which is exactly how a non-burner stays on schedule. And he understands where the soft spots in zone live and settles into them. None of it is creative or flashy, and that's a fair note on him: the routes are workmanlike, not inventive.

## Great hands, bad contested numbers

Moore has great hands, and he also loses at the catch point constantly. Now, how can both of those things be true at once? His game only makes sense once you split them apart.

The hands themselves are clean: full extension, attacking the ball away from his frame, reliable in space, with some spectacular reach catches on tape. The contested record is grim: six of eighteen last season, which at 6'3" looks like a typo. The answer is play strength. Coverage bumps Moore off his spots before the ball ever arrives. The catch never gets to be about his hands, because the positioning battle was already lost a beat earlier, through contact he should be too big to feel this much. Catching the ball and holding your ground are different skills, and right now he only has the first one.

That diagnosis is why I stay warm on him despite the ugly contested number. A hands problem at 20 would scare me. A strength problem at 20, on a 6'3" frame that's been listed as low as 195, is something a nutrition plan and two offseasons can fix. The frame has obvious room, and every failure he has routes back to the same missing pounds.

## The athletic ceiling

Now for the part nobody can coach: he's not a special athlete. Nothing on his film argues otherwise, and I won't either. The speed is build-up, the burst is functional, and his long legs make the top gear arrive late rather than never. None of that stops a technician from starting NFL games, but it does make it hard to call him a future difference-maker until he proves otherwise.

You could read him as close to a finished product, a known quantity whose curve has already flattened. I understand that concern, and it's fair. I just lean the other way: a 20-year-old with this much craft and this much frame room is exactly the profile that jumps a tier when the mass arrives, and betting against players who are already good at the hard parts is historically bad business.

Some context for the numbers, whenever you look them up: he was the second option in his own offense behind Bryant Wesco Jr., which capped his target quality. And the physicality complaint is about his own routes, not his willingness; his blocking effort and fundamentals are already fine.

## NFL translation

The projection is a starter-caliber outside receiver whose margin is craft: releases, spacing, timing, catching in space. The versatile alignment history helps the case; he's played outside, inside, on and off the line. The realistic early-NFL role is a trusted second or third receiver who wins on schedule. The ceiling question is purely athletic: even with the mass, is there enough juice for more than that? I don't have an answer yet.

## What I'm watching in 2026

The main thing I want to see this season: does added mass convert the contested losses into wins, or is the play-strength issue about something more than pounds?

I'm watching the scale first, whatever number Clemson lists him at in August, and then the collision reps: does contact still move him off his stem, and do the contested balls start splitting even? Second, the press craft against the best corners on the schedule, because that skill is his NFL meal ticket and it should show up weekly. And any verified athletic testing, whenever it comes, matters extra in his case: it will either confirm the ceiling or remove it.

A visibly stronger Moore winning half his contested chances moves him up a tier, because the craft is already there waiting. The same losses at the same weight would confirm the ceiling and shift his projection toward a complementary role for good. And a surprise in the speed department, in either direction, would change the part of this evaluation I'm least sure about.
