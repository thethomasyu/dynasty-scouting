# Jeremiah Smith

*Ohio State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Preseason evaluations are supposed to move, and this one will. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Jeremiah Smith has been the most famous receiver in college football since his freshman year. Number one overall recruit in his class, first-team All-American twice in two tries, and the word "generational" has attached itself to him so firmly it might as well be on his birth certificate. The football world made up its mind about this guy a long time ago.

Which puts a scouting profile in a strange spot. My usual job with a hyped prospect is to go hunting for the cracks, and I want to be upfront about how the hunt went: the case is about as clean as these things get. I'm not going to invent a bust argument to make this read balanced, because the evidence isn't balanced. What I can do is separate the part of his game that needs no evaluation from the one question that does, walk through both fair readings of that question, and lay out what 2026 can actually settle.

## Start with the part nobody argues about

He's listed around 6'3" and 225 pounds, and he plays with speed that isn't supposed to come in that package. He explodes out of his stance, he has real vertical speed rather than build-up speed, and his strides eat so much grass that a backpedaling corner is losing the race before he finishes turning around. Two seasons of college corners have taken their shot, and you can count the ones who held up without needing a second finger. Some of them look beaten before the snap.

The workout-warrior label doesn't fit, though, because the downfield finishing is as real as the running. He's strong at the catch point, wins the ball in the air, and finishes through contact. His 2025 tape has a catch in traffic where he absorbs a car crash of a hit and the ball never moves, and a back-shoulder finish where the hands stay down until the last beat. Late hands matter more than they sound like they should: if a receiver's arms come up early, the corner gets a free timer telling him exactly when to play the ball. Keep them down and the corner is guessing until it's too late. He tracks the deep ball naturally too, including on throws that force him to adjust mid-flight.

No verified testing exists yet, so the athletic evaluation rests entirely on film. For what it's worth, the film reads like a low-4.4 receiver at 225-plus pounds, and his high school track resume (state titles in the hurdles) says the explosion is no camera trick. If he ever runs at a combine, that's the bar he'll be measured against.

The lazy comp is Julio Jones, and it fails for a reason that matters to this profile: Julio came out with route detail. The closer stylistic pointer is Andre Johnson, a physically overwhelming X receiver who won the ball in the air and outran the safety help. That comp describes how Smith wins today. It says nothing about how his career goes, and it also quietly names the gap this profile is actually about.

## The actual evaluation: refinement

Here's the whole scouting question in one sentence: his route running sits below his athleticism, and the disagreement worth having is about how much that matters.

The mechanism behind the gap is almost sympathetic. He has never needed the fine-grain stuff. When you can outrun and out-muscle every person assigned to you since high school, nothing ever forces the details to develop. So the details show it. He runs too many routes at one speed, and pacing (changing speeds inside a route, selling vertical before throttling into the break, so the corner can never calibrate) is the single biggest lever left for converting his athletic edge into maximum separation. A one-speed route is easier to read even when the one speed is blazing. His breaks are explosive rather than crisp: violent out of the cut, but without the clean stop that creates instant space. On hitches and comebacks, the routes that live on sharp deceleration, he has real trouble separating at the break itself. Cam Coleman, in this same class, is cleanly better on that route family right now.

Watch what he does about it, though, because it's my favorite detail in his file. When the break doesn't win, he doesn't stand around waiting on the ball. He attacks back downfield toward the throw, shrinking the window a driving corner needs to undercut it. That habit protects his quarterback from interceptions, and it reads like a player who knows exactly which part of his game hasn't caught up yet.

So how much should the refinement gap cost him? One fair version says: minor nitpick. Stopping and starting at 225 pounds is physics, some of this is the tax of the frame, and a gap between his routes and his talent is the most normal thing on the file. Another fair version says: this is the thing that slows his first NFL seasons, because if a team hands him a full route tree in September of his rookie year, pro corners will make him live in the part of his game that's still under construction.

I land closer to the first version, with the second one's warning taken seriously. Every item in the concern column is technique and habit. None of it is effort, and none of it is physical. This is a player who runs full-speed decoy routes when the ball is going somewhere else, and players with that motor and this much athletic margin usually get the details eventually. The real argument is about the timeline, and a timeline question is a much better problem than a ceiling question.

## The contact habit

There's one more thread worth pulling, because it's subtler than the route stuff. At the line of scrimmage, press coverage mostly isn't a problem: he has the strength to run through a jam and enough variety not to need it every time. The wrinkle shows up deeper into routes. When coverage turns physical mid-route, he sometimes initiates the collision himself, dropping a shoulder into the defender, and the contact knocks his own route off schedule. It's a habit you build by winning every strength contest of your life. The collision always worked, so it became the answer.

The NFL will test that answer. Pro corners will happily invite the contact, absorb it, and let the route die while the officials keep their flags in their pockets. The fix is technique (hands and lean, staying on schedule through contact), and I'd bet on it getting coached out for the same motor-and-margin reasons as the routes. But it's the specific thing I'll be watching the first time somebody plays him physical for sixty minutes.

## The rest of the file

After the catch, his method is big and fast, and that's the whole method. No wiggle, no open-field creativity. Big and fast still forced fifteen missed tackles last season, because big and fast at his level is its own argument. Just don't project a YAC creator; project a battering ram with a head start.

Blocking is willing and unfinished: real flashes of perimeter impact, inconsistent execution, including one badly missed reach block last year that turned directly into a defensive touchdown. And nothing on film suggests a hands problem: big, natural catcher, no drop pattern on record. No drop data exists yet either way, so that part stays a film read.

## NFL translation

The vertical and catch-point dominance travel on day one. That package alone makes him an immediate outside starter, and the position is the least interesting question on the file: he's an X. The conditional part is the shape of the early career. Deployed first as a deep and contested-catch weapon while the route detail catches up, the transition looks smooth. Asked to be a full-tree number one immediately, he'll have stretches where the refinement gap shows in ways it never did in college. That's the honest range of outcomes, and notice it's a range about the first two years rather than about the destination.

## What 2026 can settle

The biggest question entering the season: does the fine-grain route work start showing up, and what happens the first time a corner disrupts him with technique instead of muscle?

Specifically, I'm watching four things. Whether he changes speeds inside routes instead of running everything at one blazing gear. Whether hitches and comebacks start winning at the break instead of being rescued by his work back to the ball. How he answers mid-route physicality: another shoulder, or hands and leverage. And whether the blocking execution catches up to the effort.

## What would move this evaluation

Downward: another season of one-speed routes and shoulder-first answers wouldn't make him a bad prospect, but it would push the slow-start reading from possible toward likely, and it would mean two more years of dominance taught him nothing he'll need in November NFL games. Upward: visible tempo manipulation and stop routes that win at the break would close the only real gap in the file, and then the ceiling conversation stops sounding like hype and starts sounding like arithmetic. Verified testing would matter too, in either direction, because right now the athletic tier is a film verdict with no numbers behind it.
