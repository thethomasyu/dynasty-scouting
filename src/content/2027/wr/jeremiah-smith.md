# Jeremiah Smith

*Ohio State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Preseason evaluations are supposed to move, and this one will. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Jeremiah Smith has been the most famous receiver in college football since he was a freshman. Number one overall recruit in his class, first-team All-American in both of his seasons, and people have been calling him "generational" for so long that the word barely registers as praise anymore. Everyone made up their mind about this guy a long time ago.

Which puts me in a weird spot, because my favorite thing to do with a hyped prospect is go hunting for the cracks, and I'll be upfront with you: I didn't find much. I'm not going to invent a bust case just so this profile feels balanced, because the evidence just isn't there. What I can do is go through what he does well, spend some real time on the one question I do have about his game, and tell you what the 2026 season can actually settle.

## The athletic profile

He's listed around 6'3" and 225 pounds, and he plays with speed that has no business coming in that package. He explodes off the line, the vertical speed is real speed rather than build-up speed, and his strides cover so much ground that a backpedaling corner is losing the race before he even finishes turning around. Two seasons' worth of college corners have taken their shot at him. You can just about count the ones who held up on one hand, and some of them look beaten before the snap.

He's not a workout warrior either, because the downfield finishing is just as real as the running. He's strong at the catch point, wins the ball in the air, and finishes through contact. There's a catch on his 2025 tape where he absorbs a car crash of a hit in traffic and the ball never moves, and a back-shoulder finish where his hands stay down until the last possible beat. That late-hands habit matters more than people realize. If a receiver's arms come up early, the corner gets a free timer telling him exactly when to play the ball. Keep them down and the corner is guessing until it's too late. He tracks the deep ball naturally too, including on throws that force him to adjust mid-flight.

No verified testing exists yet, so the whole athletic evaluation is a film call. For what it's worth, the film says low-4.4 receiver at 225-plus pounds, and his high school track résumé (state titles in the hurdles) says the explosion is not a camera trick. If he ever runs at a combine, that's the bar he'll be measured against.

The comp everyone reaches for is Julio Jones, and I don't like it: Julio came out of college with real route detail. The comp I keep coming back to is Andre Johnson, a physically overwhelming X receiver who won the ball in the air and outran the safety help. Remember, a comp describes how a guy wins today, never how his career turns out. And it points straight at the biggest gap in his game, which I'll get into next.

## The route running

His route running is behind his athleticism, and the thing I keep going back and forth on is how much that actually matters.

I have some sympathy for how the gap happened. He has never needed the fine-grain stuff. When you can outrun and out-muscle every person assigned to you since high school, nothing ever forces the details to develop. So the details show it. He runs too many routes at one speed. What do I mean by that? Pacing is changing speeds inside a route, selling vertical and then throttling down into the break, so the corner can never get calibrated to you. It's the biggest improvement Smith has left if he wants to turn his athletic edge into maximum separation, because a one-speed route is easier to read even when the one speed is blazing. His breaks are explosive rather than crisp: violent coming out of the cut, but without the clean stop that creates instant space. On hitches and comebacks, the routes that live entirely on sharp deceleration, he has real trouble separating at the break itself. Cam Coleman, in this same class, is just better on that route family right now.

But watch what Smith does when the break doesn't win, because it's my favorite detail on his tape. He doesn't stand around waiting for the ball. He attacks back downfield toward the throw, which shrinks the window a driving corner needs to undercut it. That habit saves his quarterback from interceptions, and it looks like a player who knows exactly which part of his game hasn't caught up yet.

So how much should the route stuff cost him? Part of me wants to say barely anything. Stopping and starting at 225 pounds is physics, some of this is just the cost of the frame, and a gap between the routes and the talent is the most normal thing you could find on a player like this. At the same time, I understand the concern that this is exactly what slows his first NFL seasons. If a team hands him a full route tree in September of his rookie year, pro corners will make him live in the part of his game that's still under construction.

If I had to pick, I lean toward barely anything, but I do take the concern seriously. Everything I just listed is technique and habit. None of it is effort, and none of it is physical. This is a guy who runs full-speed decoy routes when the ball is going somewhere else, and players with that motor and this much athletic margin usually get the details eventually. So my question with him is about the timeline, not the ceiling, and I can live with that.

## The contact habit

There's one more thread worth pulling, and it's subtler than the route stuff. Press coverage at the line mostly isn't a problem: he's strong enough to run through a jam and has enough release variety that he doesn't need to. The wrinkle shows up deeper into routes. When coverage gets physical mid-route, Smith will sometimes start the collision himself, dropping a shoulder into the defender, and the contact knocks his own route off schedule. It's a habit you build by winning every strength contest of your life. Starting the contact has always worked for him, so he keeps going back to it.

I don't think that keeps working in the NFL. Pro corners will happily invite the contact, absorb it, and let the route die while the officials keep their flags in their pockets. The fix is technique, hands and lean, staying on schedule through contact instead of stopping to win a shoving match that doesn't get him open. I'd bet on it getting coached out for the same reasons as the route details: the motor and the athletic margin are both there. But it's the specific thing I'll be watching the first time somebody plays him physical for sixty minutes.

## The rest of his game

After the catch, he's just big and fast. No wiggle, no open-field creativity. Big and fast still forced fifteen missed tackles last season, because at his size and speed it doesn't take much more. Just don't project a YAC creator. He's a battering ram with a head start.

Blocking is willing but unfinished. There are real flashes of perimeter impact, and there are reps where the execution just isn't there, including a badly missed reach block last year that turned directly into a defensive touchdown. And I have nothing bad to say about his hands: big, natural catcher, no drop pattern anywhere on his film. No drop data exists yet either way, so that part stays a film read for now.

## NFL translation

The vertical and catch-point package travels on day one, and that alone makes him an immediate outside starter. Where he lines up is easy: he's an X. The part I'm less sure about is what his first couple of seasons look like. If a team deploys him first as a deep and contested-catch weapon while the route detail catches up, the transition looks smooth. If they ask him to be a full-tree number one immediately, he'll have stretches where the refinement gap shows up in ways it never did in college. Once again, that range is about the first two years, not about the destination.

## What 2026 can settle

Entering the season, what I mostly want to see is whether the fine-grain route work starts showing up, and what happens the first time a corner disrupts him with technique instead of muscle.

Specifically, I'm watching four things. Does he start changing speeds inside routes instead of running everything at one blazing gear? Do hitches and comebacks start winning at the break instead of getting rescued by his work back to the ball? When coverage gets physical with him mid-route, does he answer with another shoulder or with hands and leverage? And does the blocking execution catch up to the effort? The effort was never the issue.

## What would move this evaluation

Downward: another season of one-speed routes and shoulder-first contact wouldn't make him a bad prospect, but it would move the slow-start scenario from possible to likely, and it would tell me that two more years of dominance taught him nothing he'll need at the next level. Upward: visible tempo manipulation, and stop routes that win at the break. That would close the only real gap in his game, and at that point I'd stop calling the ceiling talk hype and just call it accurate. Verified testing would matter too, in either direction, because right now the athletic grade is a film call with no numbers behind it.
