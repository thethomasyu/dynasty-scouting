# Jeremiah Smith

*Ohio State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his junior season. Preseason evaluations are supposed to move, and this one will. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Jeremiah Smith has been the most famous receiver in college football since he was a freshman. Number one overall recruit in his class, first-team All-American in both of his seasons, and people have been calling him "generational" for so long that the word barely registers as praise anymore. Everyone made up their mind about this guy a long time ago.

Which puts me in a weird spot, because my favorite thing to do with a hyped prospect is go hunting for the cracks. I went hunting, and I'll be upfront with you: I didn't find much. I'm not going to invent a bust case just so this profile feels balanced, because the evidence isn't balanced. What I can do is separate the part of his game that doesn't need evaluating from the one question that does, walk through both fair readings of that question, and tell you what the 2026 season can actually settle.

## The part nobody argues about

He's listed around 6'3" and 225 pounds, and he plays with speed that has no business coming in that package. He explodes off the line, the vertical speed is real speed rather than build-up speed, and his strides cover so much ground that a backpedaling corner is losing the race before he even finishes turning around. Two seasons' worth of college corners have taken their shot at him. You can just about count the ones who held up on one hand, and some of them look beaten before the snap.

He's not a workout warrior either, because the downfield finishing is just as real as the running. He's strong at the catch point, wins the ball in the air, and finishes through contact. There's a catch on his 2025 tape where he absorbs a car crash of a hit in traffic and the ball never moves, and a back-shoulder finish where his hands stay down until the last possible beat. That late-hands habit matters more than people realize. If a receiver's arms come up early, the corner gets a free timer telling him exactly when to play the ball. Keep them down and the corner is guessing until it's too late. He tracks the deep ball naturally too, including on throws that force him to adjust mid-flight.

No verified testing exists yet, so the whole athletic evaluation is a film call. For what it's worth, the film says low-4.4 receiver at 225-plus pounds, and his high school track résumé (state titles in the hurdles) says the explosion is not a camera trick. If he ever runs at a combine, that's the bar he'll be measured against.

The comp everyone reaches for is Julio Jones, and I don't like it, for a reason that matters to this profile: Julio came out of college with real route detail. The comp I keep coming back to is Andre Johnson, a physically overwhelming X receiver who won the ball in the air and outran the safety help. Remember, a comp describes how a guy wins today, never how his career turns out. And this one happens to point straight at the gap the rest of this profile is about.

## The route running

Here's the entire scouting question in one sentence: his route running is behind his athleticism, and the real argument is about how much that matters.

I have some sympathy for how the gap happened. He has never needed the fine-grain stuff. When you can outrun and out-muscle every person assigned to you since high school, nothing ever forces the details to develop. So the details show it. He runs too many routes at one speed. What do I mean by that? Pacing is changing speeds inside a route, selling vertical and then throttling down into the break, so the corner can never get calibrated to you. It's the single biggest lever Smith has left for converting his athletic edge into maximum separation, because a one-speed route is easier to read even when the one speed is blazing. His breaks are explosive rather than crisp: violent coming out of the cut, but without the clean stop that creates instant space. On hitches and comebacks, the routes that live entirely on sharp deceleration, he has real trouble separating at the break itself. Cam Coleman, in this same class, is flat-out better on that route family right now.

But watch what Smith does when the break doesn't win, because it's my favorite detail on his tape. He doesn't stand around waiting for the ball. He attacks back downfield toward the throw, which shrinks the window a driving corner needs to undercut it. That habit saves his quarterback from interceptions, and it looks like a player who knows exactly which part of his game hasn't caught up yet.

So how much should the route stuff cost him? One fair reading says: barely anything. Stopping and starting at 225 pounds is physics, some of this is just the cost of the frame, and a gap between the routes and the talent is the most normal thing you could find on a player like this. The other fair reading says: this is the thing that slows his first NFL seasons, because if a team hands him a full route tree in September of his rookie year, pro corners will make him live in the part of his game that's still under construction.

I land closer to the first reading, and I take the second one's warning seriously. Everything in the concern column is technique and habit. None of it is effort, and none of it is physical. This is a guy who runs full-speed decoy routes when the ball is going somewhere else, and players with that motor and this much athletic margin usually get the details eventually. The real argument is about the timeline, and I'd much rather argue about a timeline than a ceiling.

## The contact habit

There's one more thread worth pulling, and it's subtler than the route stuff. Press coverage at the line mostly isn't a problem: he's strong enough to run through a jam and has enough release variety that he doesn't need to. The wrinkle shows up deeper into routes. When coverage gets physical mid-route, Smith will sometimes start the collision himself, dropping a shoulder into the defender, and the contact knocks his own route off schedule. It's a habit you build by winning every strength contest of your life. The collision always worked, so it became the answer.

The NFL will test that answer. Pro corners will happily invite the contact, absorb it, and let the route die while the officials keep their flags in their pockets. The fix is technique, hands and lean, staying on schedule through contact instead of stopping to win a fight nobody is scoring. I'd bet on it getting coached out for the same motor-and-margin reasons as the routes. But it's the specific thing I'll be watching the first time somebody plays him physical for sixty minutes.

## The rest of his game

After the catch, his method is big and fast, and that's the whole method. No wiggle, no open-field creativity. Big and fast still forced fifteen missed tackles last season, because big and fast at his level is its own argument. Just don't project a YAC creator. You're getting a battering ram with a head start.

Blocking is willing but unfinished. There are real flashes of perimeter impact, and there are reps where the execution just isn't there, including a badly missed reach block last year that turned directly into a defensive touchdown. And I have nothing bad to say about his hands: big, natural catcher, no drop pattern anywhere on his film. No drop data exists yet either way, so that part stays a film read for now.

## NFL translation

The vertical and catch-point package travels on day one, and that alone makes him an immediate outside starter. Where he lines up is the least interesting question in this profile: he's an X. The conditional part is what his first couple of seasons look like. If a team deploys him first as a deep and contested-catch weapon while the route detail catches up, the transition looks smooth. If they ask him to be a full-tree number one immediately, he'll have stretches where the refinement gap shows up in ways it never did in college. Notice that's a range about the first two years, not about the destination.

## What 2026 can settle

The biggest question entering the season: does the fine-grain route work start showing up, and what happens the first time a corner disrupts him with technique instead of muscle?

Specifically, I'm watching four things. Whether he changes speeds inside routes instead of running everything at one blazing gear. Whether hitches and comebacks start winning at the break instead of getting rescued by his work back to the ball. How he answers mid-route physicality: another shoulder, or hands and leverage. And whether the blocking execution catches up to the effort, because the effort was never the issue.

## What would move this evaluation

Downward: another season of one-speed routes and shoulder-first answers wouldn't make him a bad prospect, but it would move the slow-start scenario from possible to likely, and it would tell me that two more years of dominance taught him nothing he'll need in November NFL games. Upward: visible tempo manipulation, and stop routes that win at the break. That would close the only real gap in his game, and at that point I'd stop calling the ceiling talk hype, because it would just be an accurate description. Verified testing would matter too, in either direction, because right now the athletic tier is a film verdict with no numbers behind it.
