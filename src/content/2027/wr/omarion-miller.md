# Omarion Miller

*Arizona State, by way of Colorado. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his senior season. An older prospect with plenty still unproven. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Omarion Miller's college career has been a mess that had very little to do with him: three years at Colorado under constant quarterback churn, a broken leg that ended his 2024 season in October, then a 2025 comeback good enough that he nearly declared for the last draft. Now he's a senior-year transfer at Arizona State. Through all of it, the one thing that never wavered was the body. He's a dense 210 pounds at 6'1" or 6'2", heavy for the position, and he moves in a way that makes you double-check the listing.

The body was never the question with him, though. What I'm still trying to work out is what's missing from his game, and depending on the answer you end up with two completely different careers. That question matters more to me than any single trait he has, so it gets the middle of this profile.

## The tools

The explosiveness is the foundation of everything. At his weight, which sits in the 71st percentile for the position, Miller's burst and gliding stride are special rather than merely good, and the two-sport background backs it up: he scored 26 points a game as a high school basketball player. This is the kind of athlete who was going to stand out in whichever sport he picked.

He's also comfortable in traffic in a way big athletes often aren't. His contested-catch rate ran above 60% last season, and you can see the comfort on film: he'll adjust, absorb the contact, and finish with people hanging on him. One thing to note with that number, though: part of his contested volume exists because he wasn't separating downfield. Winning those balls is a real skill, but needing to win that many of them points right back at the long-speed question, which is still unanswered. I don't doubt the burst at all, but the top gear is undocumented, and no measurement exists either way.

After the catch he's sturdy and fluid at the same time. He absorbs the first hit and keeps going, and there's a fair argument his after-catch numbers undersell the skill. The catch column has a wart, though: a double-digit drop rate, driven by concentration, looking upfield before the ball is put away. His hard-catch tape is better than his easy-catch tape, which is fixable and also slightly maddening.

## The route problem, two ways to read it

It's obvious on film that the route nuance sits below the athleticism. What I'm still on the fence about is how deep the gap goes, because his whole projection comes down to that.

The concern is that the nuance just isn't there, anywhere: he's a big, strong, explosive athlete who has never had to sell a route in his life, because size and explosion won every rep that mattered from ninth grade on. If that's the case, the whole deception layer has to be built from the foundation up, and receivers who start learning it this late have a long, well-documented history of never learning it. Everybody in football knows the big athletic receiver who stalled in the pros waiting on refinement that never came.

The other possibility is narrower and more interesting to me: the nuance already exists, just selectively. On angled routes, slants and posts, his head fakes are real and they fool people. The absence shows up in one specific place: he doesn't sell vertical before hard-stop routes, so corners sit on his hitches like they know what's coming, and one of those sat-on hitches turned into an interception on his tape. If that's the real story, he's missing exactly one habit, and it's a coachable one.

The annoying part is that the film supports both answers, because they're describing different route families, so I can't just wave one of them off. Is the nuance gap one missing habit or a whole missing discipline? If the stop-route sell is the last missing piece, Miller is one fix away from the class's upper tier. If it turns out to be the visible edge of a general absence, he's a multi-year project on a senior's clock. I lean toward the narrower answer, because the angled-route deception is too specific to be an accident, but I'm holding it loosely until September.

His zone feel is another one I haven't sorted out. There are two credible observations that look contradictory: settling into soft spots on some reps, drifting into coverage on others. Settling correctly and drifting while on the move are different behaviors, and he may own one and lack the other. I don't have a verdict there yet.

## Context, and the clock

The suppressed production has real excuses. Three different quarterbacks threw to him in 2025 alone, and the 2024 leg fracture cost him development reps he didn't have to spare. The medical question that matters now is simple and can't be answered until fall: does a full healthy season show the same explosion as before the injury?

The clock is also louder for him than for most of this class. He's a senior who nearly declared once already, which means the industry has effectively seen the tools and is now waiting on the consistency. And bodies like his get judged differently depending on the time of year: summer scouting is patient with big explosive builds, and that patience goes away by winter. He lands at Arizona State with a receiver-development staff that has earned its reputation, opposite Reed Harris, another tools-forward transfer, on a team that plans to feature them both. He will not lack opportunity or coaching, so at this point the excuses are used up.

## NFL translation

The role that fits today is big slot or power outside receiver whose day-one NFL value is contested catching, comfort in traffic, and after-catch physicality. That package plays immediately in a specific way: third downs, red zone, the friendly-window throws. Whether he's an every-down starter or a situational size piece rides almost entirely on the route question above. Release variety is its own open item, because college never forced him to develop answers at the line, and the pros will ask in week one.

## What I'm watching in 2026

The big one is the route question from earlier: is he missing one habit, or a whole discipline?

So I'm watching hitches and comebacks, specifically whether corners keep sitting on them or start getting sold vertical first. I also want to see his first real press answers. The drop count should move immediately if he's serious, since that's concentration. And I'll be watching the explosion itself, measured against his own pre-injury standard across a full season.

If stop routes start winning at the break in September, that answers the question the good way, and his tier changes fast, because everything else is already NFL-shaped. But if we get another season where the only routes that win are the ones his body wins for him, the projection hardens into a role player with a great frame. And a verified speed number, whenever one shows up, would settle the long-speed question for good.
