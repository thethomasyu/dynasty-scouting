# Omarion Miller

*Arizona State, by way of Colorado. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026, before his senior season. An older prospect with plenty still unproven. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Omarion Miller's college career has been a mess that had very little to do with him: three years at Colorado under constant quarterback churn, a broken leg that ended his 2024 season in October, then a 2025 comeback good enough that he nearly declared for the last draft. Now he's a senior-year transfer at Arizona State. Through all of it, one thing never wavered: the body. He's a dense 210 pounds at 6'1" or 6'2", heavy for the position, and he moves in a way that makes you double-check the listing.

But the evaluation lives somewhere more specific than the build. What Miller is physically was never the question. The question is the diagnosis of what's missing, and the two candidate diagnoses lead to two completely different careers. That split deserves more attention than any single trait he has, so it gets the middle of this profile.

## The tools

The explosiveness is the foundation of everything. At his weight, which sits in the 71st percentile for the position, Miller's burst and gliding stride are special rather than merely good, and the two-sport background backs it up: he scored 26 points a game as a high school basketball player. This is the kind of athlete who was going to stand out in whichever sport he picked.

He's also comfortable in traffic in a way big athletes often aren't. His contested-catch rate ran above 60% last season, and you can see the comfort on film: he'll adjust, absorb the contact, and finish with people hanging on him. One flag rides with that number, though, and it's worth sitting with: part of his contested volume exists because he wasn't separating downfield. Winning those balls is a real skill. Needing to win that many of them tells you something, and what it points at is the long-speed question, which is still unanswered. The burst is unquestioned. The top gear is undocumented, and no measurement exists either way.

After the catch he's sturdy and fluid at the same time. He absorbs the first hit and keeps going, and there's a fair argument his after-catch numbers undersell the skill. The catch column has a wart, though: a double-digit drop rate, driven by concentration, looking upfield before the ball is put away. His hard-catch tape is better than his easy-catch tape, which is fixable and also slightly maddening.

## The route problem, two ways to read it

Here's the split that decides his projection. It's obvious on film that the route nuance sits below the athleticism. The argument worth having is about the shape of the gap.

The first reading says the nuance just isn't there, anywhere: he's a big, strong, explosive athlete who has never had to sell a route in his life, because size and explosion won every rep that mattered from ninth grade on. In that reading, the whole deception layer has to be built from the foundation up, and receivers who start learning it this late have a long, well-documented history of never learning it. Everybody in football knows the big athletic receiver who stalled in the pros waiting on refinement that never came.

The second reading is narrower and more interesting: the nuance already exists, selectively. On angled routes, slants and posts, his head fakes are real and they fool people. The absence shows up in one specific place: he doesn't sell vertical before hard-stop routes, so corners sit on his hitches like they know what's coming, and one of those sat-on hitches turned into an interception on his tape. In this reading there's exactly one missing habit, and it's a coachable one.

Both readings describe the same film, because they're describing different route families. That's what makes this a real open question instead of a disagreement to wave off. Is the nuance gap one missing habit or a whole missing discipline? If the stop-route sell is the last missing piece, Miller is one fix away from the class's upper tier. If it's the visible edge of a general absence, he's a multi-year project on a senior's clock. I lean toward the narrow reading, because the angled-route deception is too specific to be an accident, but I'm holding it loosely until September.

One adjacent thread stays open too. His zone feel produces two credible observations that look contradictory: settling into soft spots on some reps, drifting into coverage on others. Settling correctly and drifting while on the move are different behaviors, and he may own one and lack the other. I don't have a verdict there yet.

## Context, and the clock

The suppressed production has real excuses. Three different quarterbacks threw to him in 2025 alone, and the 2024 leg fracture cost him development reps he didn't have to spare. The medical question that matters now is simple and can't be answered until fall: does a full healthy season show the same explosion as before the injury?

The clock is also louder for him than for most of this class. He's a senior who nearly declared once already, which means the industry has effectively seen the tools and is now waiting on the consistency. And there's a seasonal rhythm to how bodies like his get judged: summer scouting extends patience to big explosive builds that winter scouting takes back. He lands at Arizona State with a receiver-development staff that has earned its reputation, opposite Reed Harris, another tools-forward transfer, on a team that plans to feature them both. He will not lack opportunity or coaching. Which is a polite way of saying the excuses are used up.

## NFL translation

The role that fits today is big slot or power outside receiver whose day-one NFL value is contested catching, comfort in traffic, and after-catch physicality. That package plays immediately in a specific way: third downs, red zone, the friendly-window throws. Whether he's an every-down starter or a situational size piece rides almost entirely on the route diagnosis above. Release variety is its own open item, because college never forced him to develop answers at the line, and the pros will ask in week one.

## What I'm watching in 2026

One question above the rest: one missing habit, or a whole missing discipline?

So I'm watching hitches and comebacks, specifically whether corners keep sitting on them or start getting sold vertical first. His first real press answers. The drop count, which is concentration and should move immediately if he's serious. And the explosion itself, measured against his own pre-injury standard across a full season.

A September where stop routes start winning at the break settles the diagnosis the good way, and his tier changes fast, because everything else is already NFL-shaped. Another season where the only routes that win are the ones his body wins for him settles it the other way, and the projection hardens into a role player with a great frame. And a verified speed number, whenever one shows up, would settle the long-speed question for good.
