# Kenny Johnson

*Texas Tech, by way of Pittsburgh. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Thinner file than most, and sized accordingly. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Somewhere on last season's Notre Dame tape, against a corner who'll hear his name called very early in a draft, Kenny Johnson kept doing something that barely has a name. In crowded zones, he steered his routes so that other bodies, defenders, teammates, once even an official, ended up parked in his defender's recovery path. The corner's route to the catch point got longer; Johnson's got shorter. Separation manufactured with geometry instead of feet.

That skill is rare enough that most receivers never show it once, and Johnson showed it repeatedly, in one game, against elite coverage. It's the reason his name stays in this class conversation despite a file that's still mostly unwritten.

## The plan-based game

The traffic trick isn't alone. Johnson fights press well, and in a distinctive way: he converts contact into position, taking outside leverage against a jam and turning it into inside real estate underneath. He creates his own space through defenders rather than around them. There's a corner-freeze fake on his tape, a fake corner route into a post that planted the defender like a signpost, and he threatens from both the boundary and the slot with the same skill set, which is its own versatility claim.

Add it up and you get a receiver whose wins come from a plan. Which makes his one big flaw almost poetic.

## The catalog problem

Johnson leans on a single signature shake-fake, and he leans hard. Early in games it wins. Then corners catalog it, sit on it, and the wasted steps start costing him the exact separation they used to buy. The toolbox is wider than one move, a well-bent in-route here, a nearly-executed whip route there, but nothing else is reliable yet, and reliability is the whole difference between a move and a tell.

The fork on his projection is exactly this narrow: if the move library grows, the IQ and press toughness underneath make him a starter-track slot-and-boundary piece. If the library stays at one, he's a depth receiver whose best trick has an expiration date printed on it, roughly the moment NFL corners exchange notes.

## The rest of the ledger

The inventory of what's unknown here is long. His athletic caliber is genuinely unsettled: the burst reads NFL-quick in some stretches and ordinary in others, no testing number exists to break the tie, and I've stopped pretending the film settles it. He disappears for stretches, whole quarters of invisibility, with terrible quarterback play covering some of that and the rest unexplained. There are focus drops on record, turning upfield before securing the ball. The after-catch file is close to empty once you throw out a couple of badly designed touches that told you about the play-caller, and even a reliable height and weight are hard to come by.

So this profile stays short on purpose. The one proven thing is unusual and real: a receiver who understands space well enough to weaponize other people's bodies. Around it, mostly fog.

## What would fill in the file

Watch the move library above all, first game to last: a second reliable fake by November changes his trajectory more than any stat line could. Then the vanishing acts, whether a better quarterback situation keeps him present for four quarters. Then measurements, whenever they come, because the athletic tie needs breaking.

A diversified route game makes the geometry skill the garnish on a real starter. Another one-move season makes it the whole meal, and one-trick receivers eat late in drafts.
