# Kenny Johnson

*Texas Tech, by way of Pittsburgh. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Thinner record than most, and sized accordingly. Nobody has declared for anything yet.*

Somewhere on last season's Notre Dame tape, against a corner who'll hear his name called very early in a draft, Kenny Johnson kept doing something that barely has a name. In crowded zones, he steered his routes so that other bodies, defenders, teammates, once even an official, ended up parked in his defender's recovery path. The corner's path to the catch point got longer. Johnson's got shorter. He was manufacturing separation with geometry instead of feet.

That skill is rare enough that most receivers never show it once. Johnson showed it repeatedly, in one game, against elite coverage. It's the reason his name stays in this class conversation despite a record that's still mostly unwritten.

## How he gets open

The traffic trick isn't alone. Johnson fights press well, and in a distinctive way: he converts contact into position. He'll take outside leverage against a jam and turn it into inside real estate underneath, creating his own space through defenders rather than around them. There's a corner-freeze fake on his tape, a fake corner route into a post that planted the defender like a signpost. And he threatens from both the boundary and the slot with the same skill set, which is its own versatility argument.

Add it up and you get a receiver whose wins come from a plan. Which makes his one big flaw all the more frustrating.

## The one-move problem

Johnson leans on a single signature shake-fake, and he leans hard. Early in games it wins. Then corners catalog it, sit on it, and the wasted steps start costing him the exact separation the move used to buy. The toolbox is wider than one move (there's a well-bent in-route on his tape, and a nearly-executed whip route), but nothing else is reliable yet, and reliability is the whole difference between a move and a tell.

The fork in his projection is exactly that narrow. If the move library grows, the IQ and the press toughness underneath make him a starter-track piece who can play slot or boundary. If the library stays at one, he's a depth receiver whose best trick stops working roughly the moment NFL corners compare notes.

## What we don't know

The list is long. His athletic caliber is unsettled: the burst reads NFL-quick in some stretches and ordinary in others, no testing number exists to break the tie, and I've stopped pretending the film settles it. He disappears for stretches, whole quarters of invisibility, with terrible quarterback play covering some of that and the rest unexplained. There are focus drops on record, turning upfield before securing the ball. The after-catch record is close to empty once you throw out a couple of badly designed touches that mostly told you about the play-caller. Even a reliable height and weight are hard to come by.

So this profile stays short on purpose. The one proven thing is unusual and real: a receiver who understands space well enough to weaponize other people's bodies. Around it, mostly fog.

## What I'm watching

The move library above all, first game to last: a second reliable fake by November changes his trajectory more than any stat line could. Then the vanishing acts, whether a better quarterback situation keeps him present for four quarters. Then measurements, whenever they come, because the athletic tie needs breaking.

A diversified route game makes the geometry skill the bonus on top of a real starter. Another one-move season makes it the whole act, and one-trick receivers get drafted late.
