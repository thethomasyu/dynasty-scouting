# John Mateer

*Oklahoma, by way of Washington State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Oklahoma confirmed right-hand surgery after the September Auburn game, so the 2025 tape splits into a healthy stretch and a compromised one. Nobody has declared for anything.*

Mateer plays quarterback at a sprint. Quick feet, a fast release, arm angles he invents on the move, and a runner's first instinct to take off the second his initial read isn't there. It produces highlights and it produces giveaways in about equal measure, and then there's a surgery sitting in the middle of the season that makes it genuinely hard to know how much of the bad belongs to him and how much belonged to a hurt hand. You can't grade this tape cleanly. That's the first thing to make peace with on him.

## Where the evaluations agree

The playmaking is the draw, and it's genuine. The feet are quick, the release is fast, and he escapes trouble and creates as a runner in a way the pocket passers on this list simply can't. When the structure breaks, he throws from platforms and angles that have no business working and every so often do, and the big plays are real rather than manufactured. There's a competitive fearlessness to how he plays, and on the tape it reads as a plus, not a pose.

## The open questions

The arm and the decisions are where it gets hard. His arm is adequate at best for the next level, and he compensates the way adequate arms do, by driving everything, so the soft, layered throw that a checkdown or a touch window asks for isn't in his bag yet. The bigger issue is judgment. The aggression tips over into turnover-worthy throws, and his answer to early trouble is too often to run rather than reset in the pocket and climb to the second option. That's the habit that costs him most. A quarterback who bails to his legs at the first sign of a covered read is leaving throws on the field that were there for the taking, and he does it often enough that it stops looking like improvisation and starts looking like a reflex.

## Where the evaluations split

Almost everything about the projection runs through the hand, so start there. He had surgery on his throwing hand in the middle of the season, and the worst of the decision-making clustered right around it. That timing is the entire problem. It means last year's film cannot separate a compromised hand from a compromised process, and those two carry completely different futures: one gets healthy and quiets down, the other one is who he is. A clean, healthy season is the only thing that untangles them, and it hasn't happened yet, which is why anyone claiming to know exactly how good he is right now is guessing with extra steps.

## What I'm watching in 2026

Whether a changeup shows up, some sign he can take something off a throw instead of drilling every one at the same speed. Whether a healthy hand quiets down the whole-body mechanics that the compensation seems to feed, since a hand you don't trust is a reason to throw with everything else. And whether the playmaking can happen inside structure, on schedule, without sanding off the exact thing that makes him dangerous when it isn't.
