# Sam Leavitt

*LSU, by way of Arizona State and Michigan State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. His 2025 line came at Arizona State, where a foot injury ended the season early. Nobody has declared for anything.*

Leavitt will make a throw on the move that maybe a handful of college quarterbacks can make, and then, on the very next drop, take a sack that none of them would. That's the short version of him. The arm is real and the escapability is real. So are the backward drift, the one-speed everything, and the sacks that pile up the moment a play stops having a plan. And the injury cut the year short, so the sample is thin on top of being messy. You're grading a small, chaotic stack of tape and trying to guess which parts of it are actually the player.

## Where the evaluations agree

The talent was never the argument. He drives the ball with real arm strength for his size, and he's at his most dangerous throwing on the move to his right, and that off-platform work is the thing that keeps evaluators interested even after a rough season. The legs are a functional weapon, not just an escape hatch, and he'll take a swing at the hard throw when the play breaks down. Whatever else is broken here, the tools are a starting NFL quarterback's. Nobody's questioning the ingredients. They're questioning the recipe.

## The open questions

The way he plays is the problem, and it starts at his feet. When the pocket tightens he drifts backward instead of stepping up into it, and drifting backward is close to the worst thing a quarterback can do under pressure. It deepens the sack he was trying to dodge, and it drags him off a clean platform into an off-balance heave. So a good chunk of what looks like a protection problem is really a movement problem, which is the hopeful part, because movement is coachable in a way instincts aren't.

The hands are the other half. He throws nearly everything at one velocity, and a checkdown or a five-yard hook does not want a fastball; it wants touch, and the fastball turns an easy completion into a ball that handcuffs his receiver or skips in front of him. The situational tape shows the bill: his completion rate cratered on third down and cratered worse on long-yardage, the exact spots that punish a quarterback for hunting the home run instead of taking the layup sitting right there. And when the first read is gone, the scramble drill doesn't resolve into anything. It just keeps extending, and an extended play with this arm and this decision-making is precisely where the turnover-worthy throws live. The frame and the injury history sit under all of it as a durability question that his style, all that drifting and extending and exposure, only makes louder.

## Where the evaluations split

The arm was never in doubt, so the split narrows to one question: can the drift and the one-speed throwing be coached out of him, or is that simply how he plays? That's the whole thing, and the move to LSU is the reason the file stays open instead of closing. A new staff and a new scheme are a real mechanism for change. They are not proof of one. I don't know which way it breaks, and neither does anyone betting on the new offense before they've watched it actually work on him.

## What I'm watching in 2026

Whether the backward drift turns into climbing the pocket, because that one habit drives most of the sacks by itself. Whether he'll take the outlet before flipping into scramble mode. And whether the new offense actually pulls the turnover-worthy throws down out of the tape. Underneath the football, availability is its own line item: the foot has to hold up, and how LSU chooses to use him shapes everything else about the read.
