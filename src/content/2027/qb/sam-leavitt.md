# Sam Leavitt

*LSU, by way of Arizona State and Michigan State. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. His 2025 line came at Arizona State, where a foot injury ended the season early. Nobody has declared for anything.*

Leavitt will make a throw on the move that maybe a handful of college quarterbacks can make, and then take a sack on the next drop that none of them would. The arm is real and the escapability is real. So are the backward drift and the fastball-everything habit, and the sacks that follow when a play stops having a plan. An injury cut the season short, so the sample is thin on top of being messy.

## Where the evaluations agree

The talent is not the argument. He drives the ball with real arm strength for his size, and he is most dangerous throwing on the move to his right, the off-platform work that keeps evaluators interested after a bad season. The legs are a functional weapon rather than only an escape hatch, and he will try the hard throw when the play breaks. Whatever else is wrong, the raw material is a starting NFL quarterback's.

## The open questions

The way he plays is the problem, and it starts with his feet. He drifts backward instead of climbing, which turns a manageable rush into a sack and a clean platform into an off-balance heave. The sacks are less a protection story than a movement one, and movement is coachable, which is the hopeful part.

The hands are the other half. He throws nearly everything at one speed, and a checkdown or a five-yard hook does not want a fastball. On the situational tape the cost is plain: his completion rate cratered on third down and on long-yardage, the spots that punish a quarterback for hunting the big play instead of taking the layup.

When the first read is gone, the scramble drill does not resolve into anything. It keeps extending, and an extended play with this arm and this decision-making is where the turnover-worthy throws live. The frame and the injury history sit under all of it as a durability question the style makes worse.

## Where the evaluations split

The arm was never the question. Whether the drift and the fastballs can be coached out, or whether that is just how he plays, is the whole thing, and the move to LSU is the reason the file stays open rather than closed. I don't know which way it breaks, and neither does anyone betting on the new offense before they have seen it work.

## What I'm watching in 2026

Whether backward drift turns into climbing the pocket, because that single habit drives most of the sacks. Whether he takes the outlet before flipping into scramble mode. And whether the new offense actually pulls the turnover-worthy throws down. Underneath the football, availability is its own item: the foot has to hold, and how LSU chooses to use him shapes the rest.
