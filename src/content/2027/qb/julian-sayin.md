# Julian Sayin

*Ohio State, by way of Alabama. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Nobody has declared for anything.*

There's no arguing with how accurate Sayin was last year. The number is the number. The argument is about what produced it: a genuinely quick, anticipatory passer, or one of the best supporting casts in the country doing a real share of the work for him. And the honest, slightly annoying answer is that both are on the tape, and they're hard to pull apart, because a good ball to a receiver who was already open looks a lot like a good ball to a receiver the quarterback made open. Same completion. Very different quarterback.

## Where the evaluations agree

The accuracy comes first and it's real. He places the clean-pocket ball, he throws the seam with anticipation, and the operation stays on time. He also gets more on the ball than his build suggests, and it's worth knowing how, because the how is a clue. He drives with his whole body, torquing everything into the throw to manufacture velocity the arm doesn't hand him for free. It works. It also takes visible effort, and effort you can see is effort that costs something the moment the platform isn't perfect. Past his first read he mostly keeps himself out of trouble. Nobody's calling him reckless.

## The open questions

The margins are thin everywhere you look, and they stack. He's a smaller quarterback with little room to absorb a hit or survive a bad base, and the arm is adequate rather than big, so the deep ball and the drive throw both lean on that full-body effort to get there. Put those together and you have a passer with a small tolerance for anything going sideways. The concern that matters most, though, is life after the first read. When the first answer is covered, the progression slows and the sacks start to come, and a good chunk of his best tape arrives with that first window open right on schedule. The splits carry a trace of the same thing: against ranked defenses the completion rate slid out of the seventies and the giveaways climbed, roughly what you'd see if the margin his supporting cast provides is doing quiet work under the hood. Ohio State keeps handing him a clean first window. An NFL defense is going to slam it shut and dare him to find the second one.

## Where the evaluations split

A completion number is a fact. What produced it is the entire argument, and the raw data flatly can't hand you the answer, because production is as much a team stat as a quarterback one. Strip out the protection and the early separation his receivers win at the line, and one of two things is left standing: either the accuracy holds up on its own, or it was leaning on all of that the whole time. Nobody gets to know which until he plays somewhere the margin is thinner than Columbus. That isn't a knock on him. It's just the actual state of the evidence, and pretending otherwise would be selling you a confidence I don't have.

## What I'm watching in 2026

Whether he can keep the offense on schedule once the first read is taken away, which is the one question his whole projection runs through. Whether he'll drive a throw into the middle of the field under pressure instead of sliding off it to protect himself. And how much of the production is still standing when his receivers stop winning early for him, which a full season without the current safety net finally starts to answer.
