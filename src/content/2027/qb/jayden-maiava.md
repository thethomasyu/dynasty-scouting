# Jayden Maiava

*USC, by way of UNLV. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Nobody has declared for anything.*

Maiava throws a ball that is easy to catch. He layers it into space so his receivers keep running, he keeps his sack count low, and he controls the line of scrimmage before the snap, so the offense mostly stays ahead of the chains. What nobody can settle yet is whether that soft, catchable ball is a choice he makes or the only one his arm can throw.

## Where the evaluations agree

The catchable ball is the anchor. The touch is real and the layering is consistent, and he puts throws where a receiver can run to daylight instead of stopping to adjust. He avoids sacks and shows pre-snap command, which keeps the offense on schedule and out of the long-yardage downs that punish a quarterback. He is big enough to steal a few yards without being a designed runner, and he will make the throw a third or fourth down asks for. That is a real, functional base to build on.

## The open questions

The concerns cluster where the athletic and physical limits show. His interception count looks clean, but it undersells how many throws he put in danger; the volume of shaky decisions runs higher than the number admits. Pressure takes a sharp bite out of his efficiency and exposes an ordinary burst, which is the athletic limit arriving on schedule. The hot-read timing, the sideline throw, and anything that asks him to move to his left are all unfinished. And the arm reads functional rather than explosive, which is the fact the whole evaluation keeps circling back to.

## Where the evaluations split

It comes down to one mechanism. If the touch is a choice, a passer who could drive it but prefers to drop it in soft, then he is a starter who protects the ball and moves an offense. If the touch is compensation, the only setting the arm has, then the ceiling sits about where the floor is, because a quarterback who cannot threaten a defense with velocity gets crowded the moment the league speeds up. On a Saturday the catchable ball looks the same either way. The throws he chose not to make are the ones that would tell you which, and those are not on the tape.

## What I'm watching in 2026

Whether the shaky throws come down without him going passive, since a quarterback who stops taking any risk trades one problem for another. Whether he trusts a velocity throw instead of lofting it, which is the clearest tell for the choice-or-necessity question. And whether the pressure response improves without costing him the sack avoidance that is his best trait.
