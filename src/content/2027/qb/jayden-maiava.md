# Jayden Maiava

*USC, by way of UNLV. Early 2027 class profile, written in the summer of 2026. Nobody has declared for anything.*

Maiava throws a ball that's easy to catch. He layers it into space so his receivers keep running instead of stopping to adjust, he keeps his sack count down, and he controls the line of scrimmage before the snap, so the offense mostly stays ahead of the chains. Sounds like a quarterback. Here's the thing nobody can settle: whether that soft, catchable ball is a choice he's making or the only one his arm can throw. The whole evaluation is that one question, and it's a sneaky-hard one, because the two answers look identical on a Saturday.

## Where the evaluations agree

The catchable ball is the anchor of the profile. The touch is real and the layering is consistent, and he puts throws where a receiver can run onto them and get to daylight rather than break stride to go get them. He avoids sacks and plays with genuine pre-snap command, which keeps the offense on schedule and out of the long-yardage downs that bury a quarterback. He's big enough to steal a few yards without being a designed runner, and he'll make the throw a third or fourth down asks him for. That's a real, functional base. None of it is in doubt, and none of it is nothing.

## The open questions

The concerns cluster in the same place, which is wherever the athletic and physical limits start to show. Take the interception total, which looks clean and quietly lies to you. It undersells how many balls he actually put in danger, because the count of shaky decisions runs a good bit higher than the picks let on; plenty of risky throws came down safe and never touched the stat sheet. Pressure takes a sharp bite out of his efficiency and exposes an ordinary burst, which is the athletic limit arriving right on time. The hot-read timing, the sideline throw, anything that asks him to move and throw back to his left, all of it is unfinished. And the arm reads functional rather than explosive, which is the fact the whole evaluation keeps circling back to no matter where it starts out.

## Where the evaluations split

It collapses to one mechanism, and it's worth laying out plainly. If the touch is a choice, a quarterback who could drive the ball but prefers to drop it in soft, then he's a starter who protects the ball and moves an offense, and that's a good NFL player. If the touch is compensation, the only gear his arm actually has, then the ceiling sits about where the floor is, because a passer who can't threaten a defense with velocity gets crowded the instant the league speeds up on him and the soft stuff starts getting jumped. And here's why the tape can't close it: the throws that would tell you which is true are the ones he chose not to attempt, and you cannot chart a throw that never left his hand. The film shows you what he did. This question is entirely about what he didn't.

## What I'm watching in 2026

Whether the shaky throws come down in number without him going passive, since a quarterback who fixes a risk problem by refusing all risk has just swapped it for a quieter one. Whether he'll trust a velocity throw instead of lofting it, which is the single clearest tell for the choice-or-necessity question. And whether the pressure response improves without costing him the sack avoidance that's currently his best trait.
